Kinetic Theory of Gases

Molecular Model of an Ideal Gas

  • The kinetic theory of gases is a molecular model of gases
  • The molecules of gases are much further apart than those of solids or liquids
    • Due to this, forces between molecules are negligible except when the molecules collide
  • At temperatures well above the boiling point, molecules move fast
  • Gases expand to occupy all accessible space
    • Are also easily compressed
  • Experimentation has shown:
    • Boyle’s Law: volume is inversely proportional to pressure
    • Charles’s Law: volume is proportional to temperature
    • Amonton’s / Gay-Lussac’s Law: at constant volume and number of molecules, pressure is proportional to the temperature
  • When other quantities are held constant, either pressure or volume is proportional to the number of molecules within the gas

  • The van der Waals equation of state is an improved version of the ideal gas law
  • It takes into account attractive forces between molecules and the volume of the molecules
    • Attractive forces between molecules are stronger at higher density and reduce the pressure
      • Represented by adding the term a(nV)2 to the pressure
      • n/V is the molar density
    • The volume of the molecules is subtracted from the total volume to give the remaining volume that the molecules can move in
      • Represented by the positive constant b
    • a and b are empirically determined
  • At low density, the a and b terms are negligible and reduces to the ideal gas law
  • pV diagrams can be used to examine behaviours of a substance
    • Graph of pressure vs volume
  • Isotherms are frequently plotted on pV diagrams
    • Isotherms are curves showing p and a function of V with N and T fixed
  • When the substance is an ideal gas, the ideal gas law describes the curve
    • pVpVpV﻿ = constant
    • Creates a hyperbola (rotated 45 degrees)
  • When the substance isn’t ideal, the van der Waals equation of state describes the curve
    • At high temperatures, isotherms are approximately hyperbolas
    • At lower temperatures, the curves look less and less like hyperbolas
    • The critical temperature (TCT_CTC​﻿) is the temperature at which the curve has a point with zero slope
    • Beyond the critical temperature, the curves have a hump, which indicates that increasing the volume increases the pressure
    • The humps are interpreted as a liquid-gas phase transition
      • The oscillating part of the curve is replaced by a horizontal line, showing that the pressure remains constant
      • The pressure remaining constant corresponds to boiling and condensation
      • The substance is liquid to the left of the transition region if it passes through the transition region
      • Liquid and vapour coexist in the transition region
      • The substance is vapour to the right of the transition region if it passes through the transition region
      • Since isotherms above TCT_CTC​﻿ don’t go through the transition region, liquid cannot exist past it
      • Above the critical temperature at low pressures, the gas will be as dense as a liquid but be unable to, becoming supercritical
      • Above the critical temperature at low pressures, the gas will become solid
      • The critical pressure is the maximum pressure at which liquid can exist
      • The critical point is the point on the pV diagram at the critical pressure and temperature

Pressure, Temperature, and RMS Speed

  • The kinetic theory of gases relates the macroscopic properties of gases to the motion of its molecules
    • Makes some assumptions:
      • There’s a very large number of molecules NNN﻿, which are all identical and have mass mmm﻿
      • The molecules obey Newton’s laws and are in nonstop random and isotropic motion
      • Molecules are much smaller than the average distance between them
        • bbb﻿ is negligible compared to the volume of a mole of gas
      • Molecules make perfectly elastic collision with each other and the walls of the container
        • All other forces are negligible
    • Collisions between molecules are irrelevant as collisions with random velocities give new random velocities
      • Molecular collisions turn initially ordered and non-isotropic molecules random and isotropic
    • The container is assumed to be rectangular box
    • The gases are monatomic
      • Molecules only have translational kinetic energy
    • Leads to 2 important equations
    • The total internal energy (sum of all mechanical energies of all the molecules) can be found by multiplying the average kinetic energy by the amount of molecules
    • We can use the average kinetic energy to find the root-mean-square (rms) speed of a molecule
      • Not the average or most likely speed of molecules, but easily calculated
    • The speed of a molecule is around the average, but can reach extremely fast speeds at any instant

  • A degree of freedom (ddd﻿) is an independent possible motion of a molecule
    • E.g. each of the 3 dimensions of translation
  • The equipartition theorem states that the energy of a thermodynamic system in equilibrium is partitioned equally among its degrees of freedom
    • E.g. the ⅓ of the kinetic energy of a monatomic molecule is in the x-direction, ⅓ in the y-direction and ⅓ in the z-direction’
  • The molar heat capacity at constant volume of a monatomic ideal gas can be written in terms of the degrees of freedom
  • A diatomic ideal gas has:
    • 3 translational degrees of motion
    • 2 rotational degrees of motion
    • 2 vibrational degrees of motion
      • Modelled by a spring connecting the 2 atoms, which creates SHM which has both kinetic and potential energy, each form corresponding to a degree of freedom
  • Despite 7 apparent degrees of freedom, quantum mechanics limits the rotational and vibrational energies to discrete values
    • For temperatures below ~60K, the only form of energy is translational; d=3d=3d=3﻿
    • Above ~60K, the 2 rotational degrees of freedom begin to contribute
    • From ~300K to ~600K, the rotational degrees of freedom are fully active, so d=5 d=5d=5﻿
    • Above ~3000K, the vibrational degrees of freedom are fully active, so d=7d=7d=7﻿
  • Polyatomic molecules typically have an extra rotational degree of freedom
    • d=6d=6d=6﻿ at room temperature
    • d=8d=8d=8﻿ at high temperature
  • For solids, each atom has 6 degrees of freedom: one kinetic and one potential for each of the x, y, and z directions
