Gauss’s Law

  • Gauss’s law is a law used to calculation the electric fields that result from charge distributions
    • Only applicable when the charge distribution exhibits certain symmetries

Electric Flux

  • Flux describes how much of something goes through a given area
  • Formally, flux is the the dot product of a vector field with an area
    • Measure of number of field lines passing through an area
  • Electric flux is the flux from an electric field
    • Measure of number of electric field lines passing through an area
  • As area or electric field strength increases, the electric flux increases
  • Electric flux is maximized when the surface is perpendicular to the field and minimized when the surface is parallel
    • The electric flux though an open surface is represented by the symbol Φ\PhiΦ﻿
  • Electric flux is a scalar, measured in Newton-meters squared per coulomb: N⋅m2C\tfrac{N \cdot m^2}{C}CN⋅m2​﻿
  • Electric flux is heavily related to electric field lines
  • When a planar surface is oriented so it’s not perpendicular to the field lines, trigonometry must be used
    • n^\bm{\hat{n}}n^﻿ is the unit vector normal to A
  • In order to write flux more compactly, we introduce an area vector A⃗\bm{\vec{A}}A﻿
  • The area vector of a surface has
    • Magnitude equal to area (A)
    • Direction normal to the surface (n^\bm{\hat{n}}n^﻿)
      • There are 2 possible directions normal to the surface, but any choice works as long as we are consistent over the entire surface
  • If a surface is closed, it encloses a volume
    • For a closed surface, the direction of the area/normal vector points outwards, from inside to outside
  • We can now write our electric flux through a flat surface with uniform electric field in terms of the area vector
  • The net flux is the total flux passing through a an object
    • Flux coming in is negative and flux coming out is positive
  • If a closed surface contains no charge within it, it must have zero net flux because there’s no charges for field lines to land on
    • Field lines will enter and exit the same amount
  • For an arbitrary open surface, we can approximate infinitesimal areas and add them up, creating a surface integral
  • For a closed surface, we use a closed integral sign

Applying Gauss’s Law

  • We can identify symmetries to make evaluating Gauss’s law easier
    • We can find a Gaussian surface S over which the electric field has constant magnitude
    • We can also make the Gaussian surface such the electric field will be parallel to the area vector everything, simplifying the dot product
  • Problem-solving strategy for Gauss’s Law
    1. Identify the spatial symmetry of the charge distribution
      1. Spherical symmetry
      1. Cylindrical symmetry
      1. Planar symmetry

    1. Choose a Gaussian surface with the same symmetry as the charge distribution

    1. Evaluate the electric field of the charge distribution

Conductors in Electrostatic Equilibrium

  • In the presence of an electric field (often external), the free charge in a conductor redistributes itself to quickly reach electrostatic equilibrium
  • If an electric field exists inside a conductor, it exerts forces on the free electrons in it
    • Free electrons, AKA conduction electrons, are electrons that are not bound to an atom
  • When electrostatic equilibrium is reached, the electric field inside the conductor vanishes
  • Polarization of conductors only happens in the presence of an external charge/field
  • Polarization results in an induced electric field in addition to the external electric field
    • The net electric field is a vector sum of the fields of the charge and the surface charge densities −σA-\sigma_A−σA​﻿ and +σB+\sigma_B+σB​﻿
      • A and B are the poles induced
    • Net field inside the conductor is different from the field outside the conductor
  • The redistribution of charges means the 3 contributions sum to zero
  • There is no net charge enclosed by a Gaussian surface within the volume of the conductor at equilibrium
  • In equilibrium, extra charges on the conductor end up on the outer surface no matter where they originate
  • At any point just above the surface of a conductor, the electric field is related to the surface charge density
    • r^\bm{\hat{r}}r^﻿ points outwards out of the enclosed volume
  • For a conductor with a cavity, the charge separation happens inside the conductor:
    • If a positive charge is placed inside, the negative charge goes to the center and the positive charge goes to the outside
    • If a positive charge is placed outside, the charges separate normally
  • If a conductor has 2 cavities, with one having positive charge +qa+q_a+qa​﻿ and the other negative −qb-q_b−qb​﻿, −qa-q_a−qa​﻿ goes on the inside surface of cavity a, +qb+q_b+qb​﻿ goes on the inside surface of cavity b, and qa−qbq_a - q_bqa​−qb​﻿ goes on the outside surface
    • The surface charges may not be spread out evenly
  • The equilibrium charge distribution must cancel the electric field of the external charges at all points inside the conductor
