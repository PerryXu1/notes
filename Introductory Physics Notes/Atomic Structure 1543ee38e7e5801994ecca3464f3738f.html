Atomic Fluorescence

  • Fluorescence occurs when an electron in an atom is excited by a UV photon to several steps above its ground state
    • The electron can either drop back down in a single step or take smaller steps, emitting several lower energy photons

X-rays

  • X-rays are high-energy photons created by an outermost electron dropping to the inner shells
    • Normally forbidden, but can happen if there is an inner electron missing
  • X-rays can also be produced by bombarding a metal target with high-energy electrons
    • Electrons are boiled off a filament and accelerated by an electric field into a tungsten
      • The sudden deceleration from the the collision causes the electron to emit braking radiation
    • The radiation has a wide range of frequencies
    • If an electron hits and knocks out an inner shell electron, an electron in a higher level may drop down, producing an x-ray

Lasers

  • Lasers are devices that emit light which is both coherent and monochromatic
    • The photons are in-phase and have a singular frequency
  • The gas in a laser is excited and its electrons are elevated to different energy levels
    • Most return, but some stay in a metastable state
  • When we place a majority of these atoms into a metastable state, we create a population inversion
  • We then send a photon of energy into the metastable electrons, which drops the electron into a lower-energy level
    • This emits an additional photon
    • There are now two photons
    • This process is called stimulated emission
  • Stimulated emission has a high probability of happening if the energy of the incoming photon is equal to the energy difference between the excited and de-excited states
    • Therefore, the two produced photons have the same frequency
  • The photons disturb other metastable atoms, creating a domino effect that creates laser light
