The First Law of Thermodynamics

Thermodynamic Systems

  • A thermodynamic system is a collection of objects with thermodynamic properties that are of interest
    • Surrounded by its environment/surroundings
    • The thermodynamic system can exchange heat with/do work on the environment through a boundary
  • A system is closed (or isolated) if it is completely separated from its environment
    • Does not do work/exchange heat with its environment
    • Doesn’t exist in reality unless the entire universe is the system
  • A system is open if it’s not separated from its environment
    • Does work/exchanges heat with its environment
  • A system is in equilibrium if it has the same behaviour everywhere
  • When objects are at thermal equilibrium, they have the same temperature

  • The measurable properties of the system at least include the pressure, volume and temperature and are related by the equation of state

  • Quantities associated with the amount of matter are extensive properties
    • Volume, number of moles of substance
    • Doubles if the amount of matter doubles and intensive properties stay constant
    • Dependent on the amount of matter
  • Quantities unassociated with the amount of matter are intensive properties
    • Pressure, temperature
    • Independent on the amount of matter

  • Thermodynamic work is non-conservative, as it is path dependent
    • Work done differs based on the path taken
  • The internal energy of a system is the sum of the mechanical energy of its molecules/entities
  • The kinetic energy includes translational kinetic energy, as well as energy from rotation and vibration
  • The potential energy is associated only with intermolecular interactions
    • Unaffected by location or motion of the system
  • In an ideal monatomic gas, there is no rotational or vibrational kinetic energy and no potential energy (constant that’s set to zero)
    • The internal energy is only due to translational kinetic energy
  • For other systems, the internal energy cannot be expressed as simply, but in general, an increase in temperature will cause an increase in internal energy

First Law of Thermodynamics

  • The first law of thermodynamics states that the change in a system’s internal energy is equal to the heat exchanged by the system minus the work done by/on the system
    • QQQ﻿ is positive when heat is added to the system and negative when heat is taken out of the system
    • WWW﻿ is positive when the work is done by the system and negative when work is done on the system
    • Statement of energy conservation
      • If a system is isolated, its internal energy must remain constant
  • QQ Q﻿and W WW﻿ both depend on the thermodynamic path taken between 2 equilibrium states, but Q−WQ - WQ−W﻿ (and thus change in internal energy) does not

Thermodynamic Processes

  • The thermal behaviour of a system is described using thermodynamic variables
    • Pressure, volume, temperature, number of molecules/moles
  • The thermal state of the system can change from an initial state to a final state
    • The manner in which it changes states is a thermodynamic process
  • A quasi-static process is a process that’s composed of many infinitesimally small changes so that at every instant in time, the system is in equilibrium with itself and the environment
    • Necessary for a system to have well-defined values of macroscopic properties like temperature and pressure at every step in the process
    • Well defined paths in the state space of the system
  • All processes in nature are non-quasi-static
    • The path taken by a non-quasi-static process can follow any path; it could follow the one above or a completely different one
  • A heat bath is an idealised infinitely large system whose temperature doesn’t change
  • An isothermal process is achieved by keeping the system in equilibrium with a heat bath
    • Curve is hyperbolic for ideal gases
    • Temperature is constant
  • In an adiabatic process, there is no heat transfer in or out of the system
    • Can be either quasi-static or non-quasi-static
    • When a system expands adiabatically, it must do work against the environment, which makes it lose energy and thus temperature
    • An adiabatic expansion leads to a decrease in temperature
    • An adiabatic compression leads to an increase in temperature
  • A system goes through a cyclic process if its final state is the same as its initial state
    • State properties (temperature, pressure, volume, internal energy, etc.) do not change
  • A reversible process can be made to retrace its steps
    • A reversible process must be quasi-static
    • A quasi-static process is not necessarily reversible
