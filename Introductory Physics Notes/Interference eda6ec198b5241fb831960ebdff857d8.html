Interference

  • Interference is one of the most certain indications of a wave
    • Most prominent when the wave interacts with an object not large compared to its wavelength
    • Observed for all waves

Young’s Double-Slit Interference

  • Thomas Young’s double slit experiment proved that light exhibits wavelike properties
    • Young used sunlight that passed through a pinhole on a board and then passed through 2 pinholes on a second board
  • The resulting pattern created consisted of a pattern of light and dark spots, called fringes
    • Can only be explained through interference
  • We can analyse the experiment by labelling the first slit S0S_0S0​﻿ and the 2 slits on the second board S1S_1S1​﻿ and S2S_2S2​﻿ respectively
    • Slits S1S_1S1​﻿ and S2S_2S2​﻿ are equidistant from S0S_0S0​﻿ and assumed to be so narrow that they can be considered point sources for Huygens’ wavelets
    • S1S_1S1​﻿ and S2S_2S2​﻿ are a distance ddd﻿ apart, where d≤1 mmd \le 1 \text{ mm}d≤1 mm﻿
    • The distance between the 2 slits and the screen is DDD﻿, where D≈1 mD\approx 1 \text{ m}D≈1 m﻿
      • D≫dD \gg dD≫d﻿
    • S0S_0S0​﻿ is assumed to be a point source of monochromatic light
    • Therefore, S1S_1S1​﻿ and S2S_2S2​﻿ always maintain a constant phase difference and have the same frequency
      • Zero in this case since S1S_1S1​﻿ and S2S_2S2​﻿ are equidistant from S0S_0S0​﻿
    • S1S_1S1​﻿ and S2S_2S2​﻿ are therefore coherent
    • Since S1S_1S1​﻿ and S2S_2S2​﻿ are equidistant from S0S_0S0​﻿, the amplitude of the wavelets are equal
    • When light passes through the slits, the slits act as sources of coherent waves
  • Two waves are coherent waves, if they are in phase or have a definite phase relationship
  • Two waves are incoherent waves, if they have random phase relationships
  • Monochromatic light is light with a single wavelength
  • Pure constructive interference occurs when the waves are crest to crest or trough to trough
    • The waves reinforce each other
  • Pure destructive interference occurs when the waves are crest to trough or trough to crest
    • The waves cancel each other out
  • For any given point on the screen, each slit is a different distance from it
    • Therefore, different numbers of wavelengths fit into each path
    • Waves start out in phase but may end up out of phase if they end up if the paths differ by half a wavelength
    • Waves may end in phase if the paths differ by a wavelength
    • This creates the fringe patterns
  • In general, the waves will be out of phase if they differ by a half-integral number of wavelengths and will be in phase if they differ by an integral number of wavelengths

Multiple-Slit Interference

  • We can analyse slit interference where they are more than 2 slits’
    • For modern-day applications, the amount of slits approaches infinity
  • The key element in this case is a diffraction grating
  • For 3 slits (N=3N=3N=3﻿):
    • The space between slits is ddd﻿
    • The path length difference between adjacent slits is dsin⁡θd\sin\thetadsinθ﻿
    • The path length different between the first and third slit is 2dsin⁡θ2d\sin\theta2dsinθ﻿
    • The condition for constructive interference is the same as for double slit interference: dsin⁡θ=mλd\sin\theta = m\lambdadsinθ=mλ﻿
  • When the condition for constructive interference is met, the path length between the first and third slits 2dsin⁡θ2d\sin\theta2dsinθ﻿ is automatically also a multiple of θ\thetaθ﻿, so all three rays combine constructively
    • This creates very bright fringes called principal maxima
  • When the path distance between adjacent slits is λ/2\lambda/2λ/2﻿, the adjacent slits interfere destructively, but the third ray is unaffected
    • This creates a bright fringe lower in intensity than the principal maxima, called a secondary maximum
  • In general, for NNN﻿ slits, secondary maxima occurs whener an unpaired ray is present that does not go away due to destrutive interference
    • Occurs at (N−2)(N-2)(N−2)﻿ evenly spaced positions between the principle maxima
      • For three slits (N=3N=3N=3﻿), there is 111﻿ evenly spaced secondary maxima between each principle maxima
      • For four slits (N=4N=4N=4﻿), there are 222﻿ evenly spaced secondary maxima between each principle maxima
  • The amplitude of the electromagnetic wave at a secondary maxima is 1/N1/N1/N﻿ the amplitude of the wave at a principle maxima
  • Since light intensity is proportional to the amplitude squared, the intensity at a secondary maxima is 1/N21/N^21/N2﻿ the amplitude of the wave at a principle maxima
  • As NNN﻿ gets larger, the widths of each maxima become narrower due to the closely located neighboring dark fringes
    • Since the total amount of light energy is unchanged, narrower maxima means that each maximum much reach a higher intensity

Thin-Film Interference

  • Thin-film interference is interference between light reflected from different surfaces of a thin film
    • E.g. colours created by soap bubbles
  • A thin film has a thickness ttt﻿ smaller than a few times the wavelength of light λ\lambdaλ﻿
  • Since colour is associated with the wavelength and all interference depends on the ratio of the wavelength to the size of the object involved, different thicknesses lead to different colours
  • Thin film interference, similarly to slit interference, is due to differences in path length
    • At the boundary between n1n_1n1​﻿ and n2n_2n2​﻿, light is partially reflected and refracted
    • The reflected ray (ray 1) is reflected off the top surface
    • The refracted ray (ray 2) is first refracted though the n1n_1n1​﻿-n2n_2n2​﻿ interface, then reflected off the interface between n2n_2n2​﻿ and n3n_3n3​﻿, and then refracted fully though the n1n_1n1​﻿-n2n_2n2​﻿ interface
    • Ray 1 and ray 2 are parallel by the end
    • However, in thin films, the thinner the film, the darker it gets
      • Seemingly impossible since the waves should be in phase since the path difference is negligible
    • Thin-film interference is possible because phase changes can occur upon reflection

  • For mechanical waves, if a wave travelling on a string meets the boundary to another string, where the other string has a highly linear density, the wave is reflected and inverted (180°180 \degree180°﻿phase change)
    • If the second string has a lower linear density, there is no inversion
  • For light waves, a similar effect happens, with the deciding parameter being the index of refraction
  • When a light wave reflects from an interface beyond which is a medium with a higher refractive index than it, it undergoes a 180°180\degree180°﻿ phase change
    • At an interface beyond which is a medium with a lower refractive index than it, there is no inversion
  • Due to the periodic nature of waves, the phase change is equal to ±λ/2\pm \lambda/2±λ/2﻿ in path length
  • For a thin film such as a soap bubble (water surrounded by air), a phase change of λ/2\lambda/2λ/2﻿ happens at the n1n_1n1​﻿-n2n_2n2​﻿ interface for ray 1, but not for ray 2
  • When the thin-film is very thin and the path different is negligible, the waves become completely out of phase
    • This causes destructive interference and the soap bubble to be dark
  • The thickness of the film relative to the wavelength of light is also crucial in thin-film interference
  • For light that is incident perpendicular to the surface, ray 2 travels approximately 2t2t2t﻿ further than ray 1

      • Depends on if there’s a phase change in either ray
  • The wavelength in a medium, λn\lambda_nλn​﻿, is related to the wavelength in a vacuum and the index of refraction

The Michelson Interferometer

  • The Michelson Interferometer is an instrument invented by Albert A. Michelson that produces interference fringes by splitting a light beam into two parts and then recombining them after they each travel different optical paths
  • The beam comes from point SSS﻿, which is a ground-glass plate that diffuses the light from a monochromatic lamp
    • The lamp gives off monochromatic light of wavelength λ0\lambda_0λ0​﻿
  • The light hits the half-silvered mirror MMM﻿
    • Half of the light is reflected to the side and half of it passes through the mirror
  • The reflected beam rRr_RrR​﻿ hits the movable mirror M1M_1M1​﻿
    • M1M_1M1​﻿ reflects the light back through MMM﻿ to the observer
  • The transmitted beam rTr_TrT​﻿ hits the fixed mirror M2M_2M2​﻿
    • M2M_2M2​﻿ reflects the beam back to MMM﻿, which reflects the light towards the observer
  • Since both beams originate from the same point, they are coherent
  • The reflected beam rRr_RrR​﻿ passes through MMM﻿ three times whereas the transmitted beam rTr_TrT​﻿ only passes through MMM﻿ once
    • To ensure that each beam traverses the same thickness of glass, a compensator plate CCC﻿ is placed in the path of the transmitted beam rTr_TrT​﻿
    • CCC﻿ is a duplicate of MMM﻿ without the silvering
  • With the inclusion of the compensator CCC﻿, any phase difference between the two beams is due solely to the difference in the distances they travel
  • The path difference of the two beams when they recombine is 2d1−2d22d_1 - 2d_22d1​−2d2​﻿, where d1d_1d1​﻿ is the distance between MMM﻿ and M1M_1M1​﻿ and d2d_2d2​﻿ is the distance between MMM﻿ and M2M_2M2​﻿
  • If the path difference is an integer multiple of the wavelength, mλ0m\lambda_0mλ0​﻿, there will be constructive interference
    • Produces a bright image of the point on the source is seen
    • Light from any other point on the source with the same path difference will also have constructive interference
    • This produces bright circular fringes
  • When M1M_1M1​﻿ is moved by a distance Δd=λ0/2\Delta d = \lambda_0/2Δd=λ0​/2﻿, the path difference is changed by λ0\lambda_0λ0​﻿, so each fringe moves to the position previously occupied by an adjacent fringe
    • By counting the number of fringes mmm﻿ passing a given point as M1M_1M1​﻿ is moved, someone can measure very small displacements to M1M_1M1​﻿
