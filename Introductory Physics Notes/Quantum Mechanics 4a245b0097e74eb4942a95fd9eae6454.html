The Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle

  • The Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle roughly states that the more we know about an object’s position, the less we know about its momentum, and vice versa
  • Versions of the principle exist for other quantities, like energy and time

Momentum and Position

  • Consider a free particle moving in the x-direction with constant velocity uuu﻿ and momentum p=mup=mup=mu﻿
    • The particle obeys de Broglie’s relations p=ℏkp = \hbar kp=ℏk﻿ and E=ℏωE=\hbar \omegaE=ℏω﻿

      • The particle is equally likely to be found anywhere along the x-axis
    • The particle does have a definite wavelength and therefore has momentum
    • Therefore, we are completely uncertain about its position, as it can be anywhere along the x-axis, but we have its exact momentum
      • Infinite position uncertainty, but zero momentum uncertainty
  • We can make similar statements for localized particles
  • A localized particle is a particle modeled by a linear combination of free-particle (or plane-wave) states called a wave packet
    • A wave packet is created from a series of simple sine/cosine waves
  • The wave packet has many different wavelengths, as it consists of many plane waves each with different wavelengths
    • Since it has many wavelengths, it has many values of momentum, and thus has uncertainty in momentum
  • The wave packet still has uncertainty in position, but it is confined mostly to an interval Δx\Delta xΔx﻿
  • Therefore, as we gain certainty in position, we lose certainty in momentum
  • These uncertainties are summarized in the Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle
  • The principle limits what we can know about a particle from simultaneous position and momentum measurements
    • If Δx\Delta xΔx﻿ is large, Δp\Delta pΔp﻿ is small, and vice versa
  • The uncertainty persists regardless of the measurement device
  • The Gaussian distribution gives the minimum value of the uncertainty product ΔxΔp=ℏ2\Delta x \Delta p = \tfrac{\hbar}{2}ΔxΔp=2ℏ​﻿

Energy and Time

  • There exists another kind of uncertainty principle for simultaneous measurements of the energy of a quantum state and its lifetime
    • ΔE\Delta EΔE﻿ is the uncertainty in the energy measurement
    • Δt\Delta tΔt﻿ is the uncertainty in the lifetime measurement
  • The energy-time uncertainty principle does not result from a relation similar to the momentum-position uncertainty principle
  • A quantum state that exists for only a short time cannot have a definite energy
    • The frequency of a state is inversely proportional to time and frequency is related to the energy of the state
    • To measure the energy with good precision, the state must be observed for many cycles
