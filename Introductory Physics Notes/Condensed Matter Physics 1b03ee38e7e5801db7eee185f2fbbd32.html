Covalent Bonds

  • Covalent bonds are formed when two or more atoms share electrons
  • Covalent bonds can be modelled as an electron in a double square well
    • 1 electron in the electric field of 2 protons
    • The wave function is either even or odd about the center of the two wells
      • Because the electron is equally likely to be found in each well
    • When the wells are separated by a large distance, both are in the ground state of their respective wells
      • Either a single positive peak in both wells or one positive in one well and a negative one in the other
    • When the wells are brought together, they become one
      • The symmetric (even) wave function becomes the ground state
      • The antisymmetric (odd) wave function becomes the first excited state
    • The symmetric wave function becomes energetically favorable
  • Electrons are indistinguishable
    • All the same
  • Switching or exchanging electrons does not produce an observable effect
    • Property called exchange symmetry
      • Can either be symmetric or antisymmetric, but none produce an observable effect

Bonding in Polyatomic Molecules

  • A polyatomic molecule is a molecule made of two or more atoms
    • Understood in terms of covalent bonds and hybridization
  • Hybridization is a change in the energy structure of an atom due to mixed states participating in bonding
    • Mixed states are states that are linear superpositions of others
  • Atoms can use any atomic orbital, linear combinations of electron states, to bond
    • ppp﻿ shells along coordinate axes are denoted appropriately as pxp_xpx​﻿, pyp_ypy​﻿ and pzp_zpz​﻿
    • Valence electron shells with unpaired electrons participate in bonding
