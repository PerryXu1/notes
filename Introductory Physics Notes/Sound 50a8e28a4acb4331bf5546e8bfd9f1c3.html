Sound

Sound Waves

  • Sound is a mechanical wave
    • More specifically, a pressure wave composed of oscillating molecules
  • Humans can hear between 20 Hz and 20 kHz
    • Sounds below 20 Hz are infrasound
    • Sounds above 20 kHz are ultrasound
  • Sound, being a mechanical wave, is a disturbance of matter that’s transmitted outwards from its source
    • Atomically, it’s a disturbance of atoms that’s way more ordered than their thermal motions
  • Many times, sound is a periodic wave with atoms undergoing SHM
  • Speakers produce sound waves by oscillating a cone
    • The cone transfers energy to the air mostly in the form of thermal energy
    • A small part of the energy compresses and expands the surrounding air
    • This creates high-pressure areas called compressions and low-pressure areas called rarefactions
    • This travels as a longitudinal pressure wave with the same frequency as the speaker
  • In fluids, sound waves are longitudinal since fluids have very little shear strength
  • In solids, sound waves can be transverse and longitudinal
    • E.g. seismic waves
      • Pressure waves (P-waves) travel way faster than shear waves (S-waves)
        • Used to determine epicentre
  • Sound waves can be modelled both in terms of change in pressure from average pressure and displacement of molecules:
  • Sound travels at a finite speed much slower than light
  • The wavelength of a sound wave can be measured between 2 consecutive identical parts of the wave (same displacement and slope)
    • 2 consecutive compressions/rarefactions
  • The frequency of a sound wave is the number of waves that pass a point per unit time
    • Same as that of the source
  • When a wave changes mediums, the frequency will remain constant while the wavelength changes

Sound Intensity

  • Intensity is the power per unit area
    • Measured in watts per square metre
  • A sound wave, assumed to be spherical and losing no energy to thermal processes, will decrease in intensity in a inverse square relationship as it spreads out
  • Generally, the intensity of a sound wave is taken to be the time-averaged power over the area

Normal Modes of a Standing Sound Wave

  • Since sound is a wave, it exhibits interference
    • Two crests/troughs exhibit constructive interference
    • A crest and a trough exhibit destructive interference
  • A way for two identical waves initially in phase to become out of phase is to have the waves travel different distances
    • The sound waves meet at different distances from their origin
  • The sound waves will be in phase and exhibit constructive interference if the difference in path lengths is an integer multiple of a wavelength
  • The sound waves will be 180 degrees out of phase and exhibit destructive interference when the difference in path lengths is an odd multiple of half a wavelength
  • Noise cancelling equipment cleverly use interference
    • A microphone picks up incoming noise and a fast electronic analysis is performed
    • A speaker then produces a second sound 180 degrees out of phase with the original sound
    • This creates destructive interference
  • Sound waves can resonate in a hollow tube
    • The frequencies of the resonating sound waves depend on the boundary conditions
      • Similar to standing waves on strings
  • A tube closed at one end and open at the other has a node and an antinode at its ends
    • At the closed end, the air molecules can’t oscillate much, creating a node
    • At the open end, the air molecules can oscillate and creates an antinode at the right frequency
    • A tube like this has certain natural frequencies
    • The wavelength of the first mode is 4L, since the distance from a node to an antinode is a fourth of a wavelength
    • The same standing waves is created in the tube by a vibration near the closed end and near the open end
  • The lowest resonant frequency is the fundamental
  • The overtones are the higher resonant frequencies and the harmonics are the integer multiples of the fundamental
    • E.g. fundamental is f1, f3 is the third harmonic and first overtone, f5 is the fifth harmonic and second overtone

Sources of Musical Sound

  • Some musical instruments may be modelled as tubes with one end closed or both ends open
    • Resonant frequencies are produced by waves that travel down the tube and interfere with the reflected waves
      • In open tubes, some reflection occurs due to the sides of the tube and the atmospheric pressure outside
  • The antinodes depend on the radius of the tube
    • Aren’t at the tube opening
  • The waves don’t fully expand until they’re outside the tube
    • End corrections should be added for thin-walled tubes
      • 0.6 times the radius of the tube
  • Between instruments, the fundamental frequencies are usually the same
    • Overtones and their mix of intensities give them distinct characteristics
  • Tubes open at both ends usually sound richer because they have even and odd harmonics instead of just odd
  • Wind instruments amplify tones made by lips or reeds using air column resonance
  • String instruments use resonance in their sounding boxes
  • As the shape of the sounding box gets more complex, it can resonate over a wider range of frequencies

The Doppler Effect

  • The Doppler effect is the phenomenon of change in frequency due to the relative velocity of the sound’s source
    • Doppler shift is the change in frequency itself
  • Due to the relative production of sound waves
    • If the source is stationary, all sound waves are spread spherically centred at the same point
      • This means stationary observers hear the same wavelength and frequency
    • If the source is moving, the sound waves are centred at different places
      • Creates a compression in the direction of motion
        • Wavelength is shorter
      • Less compressed in the other direction
        • Wavelength is longer
    • If the observers are moving, they observe different frequencies
      • Observers moving towards the source experience higher frequencies
      • Observers moving away experience lower frequencies
  • The greater the relative speed, the greater the effect
  • Doppler effects occur for any wave, including light
  • For stationary observers and a moving source:
    • Minus sign for moving towards
    • Plus sign for moving away

    • Plus sign for moving towards
    • Minus sign for moving away

Shock Waves

  • The Doppler shift equations only work when the source travels slower than sound
  • For a source moving towards an observer approaching the speed of sound, the observed frequency approaches infinity
  • The equation breaks down at such speeds
  • The Mach number represents how many times faster a source than sound
  • No sound is received by the observer until the source has passed
  • As the source approaches the speed of sound, each successive wave interferes with the previous
    • The frequency approaches infinity, since the observer observes all at the same time
    • This creates a shock wave
  • In 3D, a shock wave has a conical shape
  • Within the cone, destructive interference heavily decreases the sound intensity
  • The angle of the shock wave can be found with geometry
  • A sonic boom is created when the shock wave hits the ground
  • Aircrafts actually create 2 shock waves
    • 1 from the nose, 1 from the tail
    • Separated by the time it would take to pass by a point
  • Shock waves are an example of a broader phenomenon: bow wakes
    • Created when the source of the waves travels faster than the propagation speed
