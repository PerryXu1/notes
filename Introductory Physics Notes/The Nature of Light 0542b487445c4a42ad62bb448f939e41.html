The Nature of Light

The Propagation of Light

  • The speed of light in a vacuum is a fundamental constant of the universe equal to:
  • The speed of light through matter is less than it is in a vacuum due to interactions with the material
  • Any material has a constant that describes the speed of light in it, called the index of refraction
  • The index of refraction is always greater than or equal to one
    • n≥1n \ge 1n≥1﻿
  • For gases, nnn﻿ is close to 1 since atoms in gases are widely separated and light can simply travel through without disturbance
  • The index of refraction is given for specific wavelengths as it differs slightly between wavelengths of light
  • The ray model of light models light as a straight line called a ray
  • Light can travel from a source to another location in 3 ways
    1. Through empty space
    1. Through a medium
    1. Reflected
  • This part of optics is called geometric optics
    • Governed by 2 laws: the law of reflection and the law of refraction

The Law of Reflection

  • The law of reflection states that the angle of incidence is equal to the angle of reflection
  • The angles of incidence and reflection are measured from the normal
  • When light hits a rough surface, it is diffused (spread out) due to the many different angles created by the roughness
  • A light ray that strikes an object made up of 2 perpendicular mirrors is always reflected back parallel to itself
    • Such an object is called a corner reflector
      • Subclass of retroreflectors

Refraction

  • Refraction is the bending of light when it changes mediums
  • Refraction is governed by the law of refraction, also known as Snell’s Law

Total Internal Reflection

  • Reflection can be done using refraction
  • When a light ray hits a boundary between two materials, part of it is reflected back while the rest is refracted
    • The reflected ray obeys the law of reflection
    • The refracted ray obeys Snell’s Law
  • The critical angle is the angle of incidence that produces a refracted angle of 90 degrees
    • Only possible if the medium the light is already in has a larger refractive index that the medium the light is attempting to go to
  • If the incident angle θ1\theta_1θ1​﻿ is greater than the critical angle, all of the light is reflected back into the medium according to the law of reflection, in a phenomenon called total internal reflection
  • Corner reflectors are perfectly efficient when the conditions for total internal reflection are satisfied
  • Fiber optics use TIR to transmit light down fibers of plastic or glass
    • Used for endoscopes
  • Diamonds sparkle due to light being stuck inside due to the low critical angle and many cuts/facets

Dispersion

  • Dispersion is the spreading of white light into its full spectrum of colours
    • White light is a pretty uniform mixture of all visible wavelengths
    • Occurs whenever the wave propagation depends on wavelength
  • All waves exhibit dispersion
  • Dispersion occurs because because the refraction index also varies a bit depending on the wavelength
    • nnn﻿ increases as wavelength decreases/frequency increases
    • Red light has the lowest refractive index
    • Violet light has the highest refractive index
  • There are tables that display the refractive indices of rays as a function of wavelength
  • Light can also be viewed as a wave
  • Dispersion causes issues in fiber optics systems, as it causes the dispersion and overlapping of messages transmitted
  • In some situations, we must make our analyses using the wave characteristics of light

Huygens’s Principle

  • Huygens’s Principle states that every point on a wavefront is a source of wavelets that propagate forwards at the same speed of the wavefront
    • The new wave front is tangent to all of the wavelets
  • The principle works for all types of waves
  • Huygens’s Principle can be used to explain reflection as a reflection of wavelets and therefore the wavefront of the light itself
  • Huygens’s Principle can also be used to explain refraction as a consequence of the wavelets, and thus the wavefront, changing in the new medium and “bending”
  • Diffraction is the spreading out of waves when it passes though an opening
    • Applies to all waves
  • Since light has very short wavelengths, diffraction will only be noticeable for much smaller openings
    • A wide opening won’t bend the edges of the wavefront much
    • A small opening will bend the edges of the wavefront, resulting in a diffraction effect

Polarization

  • Polarization is the attribute that a wave’s oscillations do have a definite direction relative to the direction of propagation of the wave
    • Polarised light has all its wave’s oscillations in the same direction
  • The direction of polarization is the direction parallel to the electric field
    • Polarized light thus has the electric field being in a definite direction relative to the direction of propagation
  • Unpolarized light is made out of many waves with many random directions of polarizations
  • Polaroid materials allow only light of a certain polarization to pass through
  • The intensity of a light after it is polarized is related to its intensity before through the angle θ\thetaθ﻿ between its direction of polarization and the axis of a filter
    • Malus’s Law
  • When light is refracted, some is reflected and some is refracted, with vertically polarized light is preferentially refracted at the surface
    • The refracted light is partially polarized perpendicular to the surface
    • The reflected light is partially polarized parallel to the surface
  • The reflected light is completely polarized at an angle θB\theta_BθB​﻿, known as Brewster’s Angle
  • A polarization filter has long molecules aligned perpendicularly to the axis
    • The electrons are forced to oscillate by the electric field, which takes energy away from the electric field
    • Electrons in long molecules can oscillate more easily parallel to the molecule than perpendicularly
      • Bound to the molecule, so it can be shared and moved parallel to the molecule, but not so much perpendicular to the molecule
    • The electrons absorb EM waves with a component in their electric field parallel to the molecule
      • Much less responsive to electric fields perpendicular to the long molecules
    • Thus, components of the E-field not along the axis is greatly reduced
  • Light scattered by air is partially polarized
    • The EM fields vibrate the electrons of air molecules perpendicular to its direction of propagation
    • The electrons then radiate, which produces EM radiation polarized perpendicular to the direction of the ray, since they oscillate perpendicular to the direction of the ray
    • When you view the light parallel to the original ray, there is no polarized component, so the light is unpolarized
    • When you view the light perpendicular to the original ray, it is polarized entirely
    • When you view the light in between parallel and perpendicular, there is some polarized component, so it is partially polarized
  • LCDs are made out of liquid crystals, whose molecules can be aligned even thought they are in a liquid
    • Liquid crystals rotate the polarization of light passing through them 90 degrees
      • Can be turned on or off by the application of voltage
    • Can be manipulated to make a region darker or lighter, like in LCD displays
  • Many crystals and solutions rotate the plane of polarization of light passing through them
    • Such substances are called optically active
    • Due to asymmetric molecule shape in the substance, like being helical
    • Can be used to analyze concentrations and molecule shapes
  • Glass and plastic become optically activity when stressed
    • The greater the stress, the greater the effect
  • Some crystals can split an unpolarized beam of light into two polarized rays; said crystals are birefringent
    • Happens because the crystal has different values for its refractive index for different directions of light polarization
    • Can create 2 perpendicularly polarized beams when aligned correctly
    • e.g. Calcite
  • Some birefringent materials preferentially absorb one of the polarizations; said materials are called dichroic
    • Can produce polarization by the preferential absorption
