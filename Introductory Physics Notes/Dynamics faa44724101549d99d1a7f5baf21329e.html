Dynamics

  • Dynamics is the study of how forces affects the motion of objects and systems
  • Dynamics is founded on the laws of motions stated by Sir Isaac Newton

Forces

  • A force is a push or a pull; it is a vector quantity
  • An external force is a force that comes from outside the object/system
  • Since a force is a vector, it can be split into its component vectors or written as a magnitude and an angle
  • A free-body diagram is a diagram that shows all external forces acting on an object or system
  • The forces are represented with arrows and the object/system is represented by a point
  • Drawing free-body diagrams
    • Draw the object under consideration; if the object is treated as a particle, it should be represented at a point at the origin
    • Include all forces that act on the object
    • Resolve force vectors into x and y components
    • Draw a separate free-body diagram for each object in the problem
  • Usually, forces are grouped as contact and field (at a distance forces) forces
    • Contact forces require physical contact in order to exert a force
    • Field forces do not require physical contact to exert a force
  • Field forces depend on the presence of a field (eg. gravitational field, electromagnetic field)
  • Fundamentally, contact forces are just electromagnetic interactions
  • The SI unit for force is the newton (N)
  • One newton is the force needed to accelerate an object with mass 1 kg at a rate of 1 metre per second squared
  • A force can be described in the vector component form
    • 2 Dimensional
      • F⃗=ai^+bj^\vec{F} = a\hat{i} + b\hat{j}F=ai^+bj^​﻿
    • 3 Dimensional
      • F⃗=ai^+bj^+ck^\vec{F} = a\hat{i} + b\hat{j} + c\hat{k}F=ai^+bj^​+ck^﻿
  • The net external force is the vector sum of all external forces

Newton’s First Law

  • Newton’s first law of motion: An object at rest remains at rest or, if in uniform motion, remains in uniform motion unless acted upon by a net external force
    • An object cannot accelerate without a force
  • Mass is the amount of matter in an object or system
  • Gravitation is the attraction of one mass to another
  • Inertia is an object’s resistance to change in its motion; resistance to acceleration
  • Mass is related to inertia
  • Newton’s first law is often called the law of inertia
  • An inertial reference frame is a reference frame that moves at constant velocity to a given frame
  • An inertial reference frame is a reference frame where Newton’s first law is valid
  • All inertial reference frames are equivalent; there is no “true” reference frame
  • A state of equilibrium means there is a net force of zero
    • There are 2 states of equilibrium
    • Static equilibrium: Fnet⃗=0⃗,v⃗=0⃗\vec{F_{net}} = \vec{0}, \vec{v} = \vec{0}Fnet​​=0,v=0﻿; v object at rest
    • Dynamic equilibrium: Fnet⃗=0⃗,v⃗≠0⃗\vec{F_{net}} = \vec{0}, \vec{v} \ne \vec{0}Fnet​​=0,v=0﻿; object in uniform motion
  • Acceleration is zero (velocity is constant) when the net force is zero
  • An internal force acts between the components of a system

Newton’s Second Law

  • Acceleration is directly proportional to force
  • Newton’s second law of motion states that the acceleration of a system is directly proportional and in the same direction as the net external force and inversely proportional to the mass

Mass and Weight

  • Weight is the pull of the Earth on an object; denoted as w
  • Weight is the force responsible for the downwards acceleration of gravity
  • In scalar form:
  • When the net external force on an object is its weight, the object is in free fall
    • Due to air resistance, objects are never truly in free fall

Newton’s Third Law

  • Newton’s third law of motion states whenever an object exerts a force on another, the object experiences a force equal in magnitude and opposite in direction to the force it exerts
    • “Every action has an equal and opposite reaction”
    • When A exerts a force F on B, B simultaneously exerts a force -F on A
  • The equal and opposite forces do not cancel because they act on different systems
  • On an inclined plane, the weight perpendicular to the plane is equal to the weight times cosine of the angle it makes with the plane
  • On an inclined plane, the weight parallel to the plane is equal to the weight times sine of the angle it makes with the plane

Friction

  • Friction is a force that opposes relative motion between systems in contact
  • There are 2 types of friction
    • Static friction acts on 2 relatively stationary systems
    • Kinetic friction acts on 2 relatively moving systems
  • μk\mu_kμk​﻿ and μs\mu_sμs​﻿ are kinetic and static friction coefficients
    • Between 0 and 1
  • Static friction is usually stronger than kinetic friction
  • Static friction increases to be equal and opposite the exerted force up to the maximum
  • The net force on an object will be zero until the applied force exceeds the maximum static friction force, after which the net force will be the sum of the applied and kinetic friction forces
  • When an object slides down an inclined plane with constant velocity (net force = 0), the coefficient of kinetic friction is the tangent of the angle

Centripetal Force

  • Centripetal force is the force that causes centripetal acceleration
    • Not a force by itself, but “supplied” by other forces
  • A banked curve is a curve that is at an angle, used for better negotiation of curves by vehicles
  • For ideal banking, the angle is such that you can negotiate the curve at a certain speed without friction
    • The net external force equals the horizontal centripetal force without friction
    • The horizontal component of the normal force must equal the centripetal force

  • The Coriolis force is an inertial force that causes the curvature in the paths of objects relative to a rotating object
