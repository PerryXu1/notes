Current and Resistance

Electrical Current

  • Electrical current is the rate at which electric charge moves past a point
  • Current is scalar
  • Current is measured in amperes (A)
    • 1 Coulomb per second
  • For current to flow, there must be a circuit
    • A circuit is a complete path/loop
  • Schematics are simplified diagrams of circuits
    • Uses many symbols
  • The conventional current goes from the positive battery terminal to the negative
    • However, the actual electron flow in a conducting metal (current in most circuits) is in the opposite direction
      • Negative to positive
  • Depending on the situation, either only negative charges, only positive charges, or both move
  • Electric fields are present in conductors and produce the current
  • For static electricity, charges redistribute themselves on the surface of a conductor to reach equilibrium and cancel out the external electric field
    • However, in a circuit, the charges are prevented from reaching equilibrium by external sources of electric potential
      • e.g. batteries
  • The electric potential provides the energy needed to move the charges
    • Keeps the charges moving at a drift velocity

Model of Conduction in Metals

  • Electrons within a wire move chaotically, zig-zagging and drifting
  • Current density is used when talking about drift velocity, since current is scalar
  • Electronic signals move at a significant fraction of c, but each electron moves on the order of 10410^4104﻿ m/s
    • Electronic signals move so fast due to each electron pushing the next ones
      • Similar to a shock wave or slinky
  • Good conductors have many free charges
    • Good conductors that are metals have many free electrons
      • The electrons move almost randomly
  • The drift velocity vdv_dvd​﻿ is the average velocity of the free charges
  • Where I is the current, n is the free charge density, q is the charge of each carrier, and A is the cross-sectional area
  • For electrons, drift velocity travels opposite to the electric field and current
  • Electron collisions transfer energy to the atoms of the conductor
  • The electric field moves the electrons, doing work, but it doesn’t increase the kinetic energy of the charges
    • The work is transferred to the atoms of the conductor
      • Increases temperature usually
  • Sometimes, the velocity of an electron can be much greater than its drift velocity
    • Since atoms in a metallic conductor are in a lattice, some electrons don’t experience a strong attraction to the nucleus
      • Are free electrons; aren’t bound
      • Can move freely in a sea of electrons
      • They accelerate under the influence of an electric field
      • Collide with other atoms and electrons, increasing temperature
    • Free electrons don’t exist in insulators due to their atomic arrangement
  • The greater the diameter, the greater the current-carrying capacity
    • Wire diameter is measured in gauge
      • The larger the gauge, the thinner the wire
      • Common household wiring ranges from 10-gauge to 14-gauge
  • Current density, a vector quantity, is the flow of charge through an infinitesimal area, per unit area
    • Includes the local magnitude and direction of the charge flow
    • Measured in ampere per metre squared
    • Direction is the direction of net positive charge flow through the area
  • Infinitesimal current is the dot product of current density and infinitesimal area
  • The total current is found by integrating the current density over the area
  • The magnitude of the current density is the current per unit area
  • The current density can also be written in terms of the drift velocity
  • If q is positive, the electron drift speed is in the same direction as the electric field
  • If q is negative, the electron drift speed is in the opposite direction as the electric field

Resistivity and Resistance

  • Resistivity is a measure of how much a material resists the flow of charges
  • When a circuit is turned on, the current density depends on both the electric field created by the electric potential source, and the properties of the material
    • In some materials, the current density is approximately proportional to the electric field
  • Where 𝜎 is the electrical conductivity, a measure of the material’s ability to conduct/transmit electricity
  • Intrinsic property of a material
  • Conductors have a higher electrical conductivity than insulators
  • 𝜎 is measured in amperes per volt-metre or inverse ohm-metres (Ω*m)-1
  • The ohm (Ω) is the unit of resistance
    • 1 ohm is 1 volt per ampere
  • Resistivity (⍴) is a measure of how strongly a material opposes the flow of current
    • Measured in ohm-metres
  • Resistivity can also be written in terms of the electric field and current density
    • The greater the resistivity, the greater the field needed to produce a given current density
    • The lower the resistivity, the greater the current density a electrical field produces
  • The electric field through an electric potential source is equal to the resistivity and the current density
  • Resistivity is also dependent on the temperature
  • In many materials, resistivity is approximately linearly dependent on temperature
  • Where ΔT is the change from the initial temperature that ⍴0 is measured at; usually change from 20 C
  • Resistance is the ratio of the voltage to the current
  • Resistance is a measure of how difficult it is to pass current through a component
    • Dependent on resistivity
  • Resistors are circuit components that reduce current flow or provide a voltage drop
    • There are 2 symbols for a resistor
    • Carbon is commonly used as a resistor
      • Carbon tracks around a ceramic core, with 2 copper leads
    • Metal film is also commonly used
      • Metal oxide tracks around a ceramic core, with 2 copper leads
    • Has 4 coloured bands
      • The first 2 bands give the first and second digit respectively
      • The third band gives the multiplier
      • The fourth band gives the tolerance
        • Similar to uncertainty
  • Since resistance is proportional to resistivity, it’s also linearly dependent on temperature

Ohm’s Law

  • The current that flows through most substances is directly proportional to the voltage applied to it
    • R = IV
      • A cause-and-effect relationship, where voltage causes the current
      • A substance in which this applies is called ohmic
      • A substance in which this doesn’t apply is called nonohmic
  • Ohm’s law relates voltage, current, and resistance
  • An example of a nonohmic device is the diode
    • A diode allows current flow in only 1 direction

Electrical Energy and Power

  • Power, often associated with electricity, is the rate of work over time
  • Either the current or voltage can be subbed out to include resistance, to find the power dissipated by a resistor
  • Fuses are components that protect a circuit from high currents
    • Utilise the dissipated heat
      • The dissipated heat melts the wire portion (has low melting point) and breaks the fuse at the rated current
    • Must be replaced afterwards
  • The total energy consumed at a power for a certain amount of time:
  • If the power is delivered at a constant rate
  • Energy bills use the unit of kilowatt-hours (kWh)
    • 1 kilowatt-hour is the energy consumed by a power of 1 kilowatt running for 1 hour
    • 1 kWh = 3.6 * 10^6 J

Superconductors

  • The heavy majority of any electric current produces heat
    • Due to most conductors having some resistance
  • At the critical temperature TC, some materials become superconductors
    • Superconductors have zero resistance
    • This phenomenon is called superconductivity
  • Mercury becomes a superconductor at 4.2 K
  • In the past years, many new superconducting materials have become available
  • In a superconductor, a current persists without a voltage source once its been introduced
  • Another property of superconductivity is the exclusion of magnetic fields
    • Called the Meissner effect
    • A permanent magnet will levitate in a stable position above the superconductor
  • Superconductors can be used to create superconducting magnets
    • 10 times stronger than the strongest electromagnets
    • Used in MRIs
  • The SQUID (superconducting quantum interference device) is a magnetometer that measures extremely small magnetic fields
    • Based on superconducting loops containing Josephson junctions
    • Based off the Josephson effect
