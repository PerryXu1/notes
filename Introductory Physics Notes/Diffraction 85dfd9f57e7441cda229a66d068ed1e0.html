Diffraction

  • If you pass monochromatic light through a single slit, it spreads out to create a interference pattern
  • Due to Huygen’s principle, a wave from a slit can behave as an infinite number of point wave sources
  • These waves interfere with each other, creating an interference pattern with only one slit
  • When slits have finite width, each point along the opening can be considered a point source of light

Single-Slit Diffraction

  • Diffraction is a phenomenon where waves bend around the edges of an opening or obstacle
    • Exhibited by all waves
  • After passing though a narrow aperture, waves tend to spread out
  • Sound wave diffraction is obvious to us because their wavelengths are macroscopic
  • Macroscopic objects do not diffract light significantly due to the microscopic wavelengths of visible light
  • A single slit diffraction pattern has a central bright maximum
    • Decreases rapidly on both sides
      • Unlike a diffraction grating where the bright fringes decrease in intensity much more slowly

  • In single slit diffraction, we assume the light is uniform in intensity and phase across the width of the slit
    • All points on the light wave must emit wavelets as required by Huygen’s principle
  • We also assume that the screen distance is much greater than the size of the slit
    • We can then assume that rays heading towards a common destination are approximately parallel
  • When the waves travel straight forward, they remain in a phase and thus we observe a bright central maximum
  • When rays travel at an angle θ\thetaθ﻿ relative to the original direction of the beam, each ray travels a different distance to a common point
    • Can arrive in or out of phase
  • For angles with destructive interference, there is total pair-wise cancellation of all rays
    • The ray from the bottom travels λ\lambdaλ﻿ more than the ray from the top, so the ray from the center travels λ/2\lambda/2λ/2﻿ less than the one at the bottom edge of the slit

Double-Slit Diffraction

  • In double slit experiments, there is also a diffraction effect from each slit
    • If the slit is smaller than the wavelength, the diffraction effect is negligible
    • If the slit is widened, the diffraction effect is noticeable
  • To calculate the diffraction pattern for any number of slits, we generalize the previous method by giving each slit a uniform distribution of Huygens wavelet sources, and then summing them for each slit
  • The Two-Slit Diffraction Pattern, with slits of width aaa﻿ separated by a distance ddd﻿ is the interference pattern of two point sources separated by ddd﻿, multiplied by the diffraction pattern of a slit of width aaa﻿
    • The locations of the interference fringes are still given by dsin⁡θ=mλd\sin\theta = m\lambdadsinθ=mλ﻿, but the intensities of the fringes are scaled by diffraction effects
  • The effects of interference and diffraction operate simultaneously, creating a complex pattern
    • If the maximum of the interference is in the same direction as the minimum of the diffraction, the maximum is missing
      • Called a missing order

Diffraction Gratings

  • Many modern applications of slit interference use many many slits, which make a diffraction grating
  • As discussed in the previous section, there are N−2N-2N−2﻿ secondary maxima between each principal maxima, each with intensity proportional to 1/N21/N^21/N2﻿
    • As N→∞N \to \infinN→∞﻿, the amount of secondary maxima goes to infinity, so the width of the maxima becomes infinitesimally small
    • The intensity of the secondary maxima also goes to zero
    • This creates a pattern of only the principal maxima, which are very bright and very narrow
  • The number of slits is not infinite in real world applications, but large enough to produce the equivalent effect
  • A diffraction grating is an optical element made out glass with a large amount of carvings in precisely positioned parallel lines
    • The uncarved regions act with slits
    • Easily mass produced
    • Can have over 1000 lines per millimeter
    • Work for both transmission and reflection of light
    • Natural diffraction gratings are responsible for the rainbow effect on certain birds like the hummingbird and on some butterflies
      • Created by tiny finger-like structures in regular patterns
      • Called iridescence
  • In a diffraction grating, the central maximum is pure white, but the higher-order maxima disperse the light into a rainbow
  • Diffraction gratings are used for spectroscopic dispersion and light analysis
    • Usefulness comes from them forming a sharper pattern than double slits do
    • A grating can be used to specifically analyse a wavelength emitted by molecules in diseased cells or to excite certain molecules in a sample
  • Can be roughly treated as a double slit with the slit separation determined by the slit density
    • The slit density can be used to find the distance between each slit, and then used in double slit equation formulae

Circular Apertures and Resolution

  • Diffraction limits the detail we can obtain in images
  • When light passes through a small circular aperture, it produces a spot with a fuzzy edge surrounded by circles of light
    • The fuzzy edge is due to diffraction
    • Light from different parts of the aperture interferes constructively and destructively
    • Most noticeable with small apertures
  • For 2 point light sources that are close to each other, it can be impossible to tell that there are indeed two light sources rather than one
  • Diffraction limits the resolution we can obtain
  • The same effect is seen in all optical elements with a finite diameter, such as lenses, apertures, and telescopes
  • The Rayleigh Criterion establishes that two images are just resolvable when the center of one diffraction pattern is directly over the first minimum of the other’s diffraction pattern
  • The first minimum is at an angle θ=1.22λ/D\theta = 1.22\lambda/Dθ=1.22λ/D﻿, which means that two point objects are just resolvable if they are separated by the angle θ\thetaθ﻿ (in radians)
    • `DDD﻿ is the diameter of the optical element (aperture, lens, mirror, etc.)
    • The angle θ\thetaθ﻿ is often known is the diffraction limit
  • The limit imposed on resolution by diffraction is fundamental and inescapable

    • The beam spreads out with an angle θ=1.22λ/D\theta = 1.22\lambda/Dθ=1.22λ/D﻿

X-Ray Diffraction

  • Since X-ray photons have similar wavelengths to atomic sizes, they can be used to gather information about atoms and molecules in a process called X-ray diffraction
  • Used to find the double-helix structure of DNA
  • We can use X-ray crystallography to gather information about crystals
  • When using X-rays in a crystal lattice, the X-rays each reflect off a different plane of atoms within the lattice
    • Results in a path length difference of 2dsin⁡θ2d\sin\theta2dsinθ﻿
    • Constructive interference results when the path length difference is an integer multiple of the wavelength, as stated by the Bragg equation
    • θ\thetaθ﻿ is measured from the surface of the object, not the normal
  • Scattering cab occur simultaneously from different families of planes at different orientations and spacing patterns
    • Called Bragg planes
    • Creates complex interference patterns

Holography

  • A hologram is a three-dimensional image record on film by lasers
  • Holography uses light interference or wave optics
    • As opposed to normal photography, which uses geometry optics
  • One method of holography uses coherent light split by a mirror
    • Part of the light illuminates an object
    • The remainder (reference beam) shines directly on a piece of film
    • Light scatted from the object interferes with the reference beam, creating a complex interference pattern
    • The film is a negative, so constructive interference darkens the film
  • A transmission hologram produces both real and virtual images when a laser of the same type as that it was exposed to is passed through it
