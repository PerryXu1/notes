Magnetism in Matter

  • What makes certain materials magnetic?
    • Magnetism on a microscopic level
  • Within an atom, every electron travels in an orbit and also spins on an internal axis
    • Both produce magnetic dipoles, as they are current loops
  • For any atom, the net magnetic dipole moment is the vector of its individual magnetic dipole moments
    • Usually given in tables
    • Some atoms have a net zero net dipole moment
    • On the order of 10−23A⋅m210^{-23} A \cdot m^210−23A⋅m2﻿
  • Matter in our macroscopic level typically has approximately 102610^{26}1026﻿ atoms/ions
  • If there’s not external magnetic field, the dipoles are randomly oriented
    • The dipoles cancel out
  • In an external magnetic field, the dipoles tend to align with the field
  • A samples response to the external field determines its classification as either paramagnetic, ferromagnetic, or diamagnetic

Paramagnetic Materials

  • An external magnetic field produces a torque on the dipoles that tends to align them with the external field

Diamagnetic Materials

  • A magnetic field always induces a magnetic dipole in an atom
    • Points opposite the applied field
  • In paramagnetic and ferromagnetic materials, the induced magnetic dipole is masked by the much stronger permanent magnetic dipoles of the atoms
  • In diamagnetic materials, the atoms have no permanent magnetic dipole, so there is no masking and the induced dipole points opposite the external magnetic field
  • Can be modelled similarly to paramagnetic materials
    • χ\chiχ﻿ is negative for diamagnetic materials

Ferromagnetic Materials

  • Many magnets are made with ferromagnetic materials like iron/its alloys
  • Ferromagnetic materials have tiny regions called magnetic domains
    • Volumes range from 10−1210^{-12}10−12﻿ to 10−810^{-8}10−8﻿ m3\text{m}^3m3﻿
    • Contain about 101710^{17}1017﻿ to 102110^{21}1021﻿ atoms
  • In each domain, the magnetic dipoles are strongly aligned in the same direction due to quantum mechanical coupling
    • Stronger than thermal agitation/collisions at room temperature
  • Each domain has a net dipole moment
  • Some materials have weaker coupling effects, and thus are only ferromagnetic at lower temperatures
  • If the magnetic domains are randomly oriented, there is no net magnetic dipole moment and the material is said to be unmagnetized
  • When subject to an external magnetic field:
    • The dipole moments of the domains rotate to somewhat align with the external field
    • Also, aligned domains tend to increase in size while unaligned ones decrease
  • This creates a net magnetic dipole moment directed along the applied magnetic field
    • The net dipole is much stronger than one found in paramagnetic materials
    • The domains also do not misaligned by thermal agitation due to their large number of atoms
    • This produces a larger magnetic field within the material
  • Only 5 elements have the domains needed to be ferromagnetic (many of their alloys are also ferromagnetic though)
    • Iron
    • Cobalt
    • Nickel
    • Gadolinium
    • Dysprosium
  • The magnetic susceptibility χ\chiχ﻿ for ferromagnetic materials usually ranges from 10310^3103﻿ to 10410^4104﻿
    • Also dependent on the history of the magnetic field to which the material has been subjected
