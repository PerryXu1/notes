Temperature and Heat

Temperature and Thermal Equilibrium

  • Temperature is operationally defined as the quantity of what we measure with a thermometer
    • Proportional to the average translational kinetic energy
  • Differences in temperature allow for heat transfer
    • Heat transfer (AKA heat) is the transfer of energy between objects with differing temperature
  • 2 objects are in thermal equilibrium if they are in close contact and are allowed to gain energy from the other, but no net energy is transferred between them
    • Objects in thermal equilibrium do not exchange energy
    • 2 objects in thermal equilibrium have the same temperature
  • The zeroth law of thermodynamics states that if object A is in thermal equilibrium with object B, and object B is in thermal equilibrium with object C, then object A is in thermal equilibrium with object C
    • Statement of transitivity

Thermometers and Temperature Scales

  • A thermometer measures its own temperature
    • Thermal equilibrium allows us to say that a thermometer measures the temperature of something else
  • In thermodynamics, systems are macroscopic, and thus consist of huge numbers of molecules
    • An object is in thermal equilibrium with itself if all parts of it are at the same temperature
  • The basis of a thermometer is any physical property that consistently changes with temperature change
    • Some common examples are volume expanding with temperature, electrical resistance, colour, and emission of infrared radiation
  • Thermometers measure temperature according to temperature scales
    • The 3 most known temperature scales are Celsius, Kelvin, and Fahrenheit
  • Temperature scales require 2 reproducible temperatures
    • The freezing and boiling points of water at atmospheric pressure are frequently used
  • In Celsius:
    • The freezing point of water is 0℃
    • The boiling point of water is 100℃
    • The unit of temperature is the degree Celsius (℃)
  • In Fahrenheit:
    • The freezing point of water is 32℉
    • The boiling point of water is 212℉
    • The unit of temperature is the degree Fahrenheit (℉)
  • In Kelvin:
    • Absolute zero is 0 K
    • The freezing point of water is 273.15 K
    • The boiling point of water is 373.15 K
    • Temperature differences are the same in kelvins and degrees Celsius
  • Kelvins are defined by terms of the triple point, the combination of temperature and pressure where all three states of water can coexist
  • Thermometers are often calibrated with constant-volume gas thermometers, which come to equilibrium slowly and are unpractical for everyday use

Thermal Expansion

  • Thermal expansion is the volumetric expansion of a material due to change in temperature
    • This also causes change in density in liquids and gases
    • Solids also undergo thermal expansion
    • Change in length (1D)
    • α\alphaα﻿ is the coefficient of linear expansion and is approximately constant
    • Change in area (2D)
    • Change in volume (3D)

      • β\betaβ﻿ is the coefficient of volume expansion and is approximately 3α3\alpha3α﻿

  • Thermal expansion happens because increased temperature is a result of increased kinetic energy, which increases the average distance between molecules
  • Most substances in normal conditions can be approximated as isotropic, meaning that there’s no preferred direction
    • An increase in temperature increases the solid’s size by a certain fraction in each dimension
    • The proportions remain the same
  • A bimetallic strip exploits thermal expansion to perform tasks at certain temperatures
  • Liquids have defined volume expansion, but undefined linear and area expansion
    • Due to liquids taking on the shape of their container
  • In general objects expand as temperature increases
    • Water is an important exception
      • Water does expand (density decreases) as temperature increases for temperatures above 4℃
      • Water is densest at 4℃ and expands as temperature decreases between 0℃ and 4℃
      • The freezing of water within a pond is an example of this phenomenon
        • When water nears the pond surface cools to 4℃, it’s denser than the rest and sinks to the bottom, leaving warmer water at the top
        • If the temperature at the surface is below 4℃, the water is less dense than the water below and stays near the top, allowing it to freeze
  • Thermal stress is stress due to changes in temperature
    • Caused by preventing an object undergoing thermal expansion/contraction from expanding/contracting
    • Can cause damage
    • To find thermal stress, calculate the expansion/contraction and use that to find the stress
    • Let the ΔL\Delta LΔL﻿ of the thermal expansion equal the ΔL\Delta LΔL﻿ of the elastic distortion (except the signs are opposite)

Heat Transfer, Specific Heat, and Calorimetry

  • Internal energy, also known as thermal energy, is the sum of the mechanical energies of its molecules
  • Heat is measured in joules, since its a form of energy
    • Heat can also be measured in calories
      • A calorie (cal) is the energy needed to change the temperature of 1g of water by 1℃ between 14.5℃ and 15.5℃
    • Kilocalories (kcal) are also used
      • The energy needed to change in the temperature of 1kg of water by 1℃
      • Food calories (Cal) are actually kilocalories
      • 1 kcal = 4186 J
  • The temperature of a substance can be changed by doing work
  • James Prescott Joule established the mechanical equivalent of heat, which is the work needed to produce the same effects as heat transfer
  • Increasing internal energy via heat transfer gives the same result as increasing it by doing work
    • Thus, despite having an internal energy, an object doesn’t have a certain “heat content” or “work content”
  • A quantity that depends only on the system’s current state and not on the history of the system is a state variable
    • Temperature and internal energy are state variables
    • Heat and work are not state variables
  • Increasing the internal energy of a system doesn’t necessarily increase its temperature
    • Phase changes are good examples of such
  • With no phase change and work done on/by the system, the transferred heat is proportional to the change in temperature and mass of the system
    • ccc﻿ is the specific heat (AKA the specific heat capacity/heat capacity) and depends on the substance and its phase
      • The specific heat is the amount of heat needed to change 1kg of a substance by 1℃
      • Measured in units of J/(kg * K) or J/(kg * ℃)
  • Transferred heat is the change in internal energy
  • Specific heat values are empirically measured
    • Water has a very large specific heat
    • The specific heats of gases depend on what’s held constant during the heating
      • Either the volume or pressure
  • Specific heat also depends on temperature, and must be defined with an infinitesimal change in temperature
  • A calorimeter prevents heat transfer in or out
  • The use of a calorimeter to make measurements is called calorimetry
    • A calorimetry problem is concerned with objects that are thermally isolated from their surroundings
  • In calorimetry problems, the heat gained by an object must equal the heat lost by the other
    • Heat gained is positive, whereas heat lost is negative

Phase Changes

  • Solid-liquid transition
    • In melting/fusion, a solid turns into a liquid
    • In freezing, a liquid turns into a solid
    • The point at which a substance melts or freezes is the melting point
  • Liquid-gas transition
    • In evaporation, a liquid turns into a gas
    • In condensation, a gas turns into a liquid
    • The point at which a substance condenses or boils is the boiling point
  • Boiling and melting points depend on pressure
    • High pressure favours the denser form
      • The main exception is the melting and freezing of water
    • High pressure raises the melting/boiling point
    • Low pressure lowers the melting/boiling point
  • A phase diagram is a pressure to temperature graph with regions for each phase
    • The solid curves indicate phase transitions
    • The boiling point curve ends at the critical point
      • The critical temperature is the temperature of the critical point
      • The critical pressure is the pressure of the critical point
      • Above the critical temperature, liquid and gas phases cannot be distinguished
      • Such a substance is called a supercritical fluid
      • At high enough pressure above the critical point, the gas will have the density of a liquid, but won’t condense
    • Water is unusual in that ice is less dense than liquid water
      • Melting/freezing can be accomplished by increasing pressure
      • The melting and boiling lines meet at the triple point
      • The phase change from solid to gas is sublimation
    • For pressures below the triple point, the substance can either be a gas or solid
  • At the melting temperature, solid and liquids phases are in equilibrium
    • If heat is added, some of the solid will melt
    • If heat is removed, some of the liquid will freeze
  • Generally, liquid and gas are in equilibrium at any temperature
    • The gas phase is a vapor if it exists at a temperature below the boiling temperature
  • In a closed container at a fixed temperature, liquid will evaporate until the pressure of the gas reaches a certain value, called the vapor pressure
    • Vapor pressure depends on the gas and temperature
    • At vapor pressure, added heat will evaporate more water
    • At vapor pressure, removed heat will cause some of the gas to condense
      • Molecules will either join the liquid or form suspended droplets
    • If there isn’t enough liquid for the gas to reach vapor pressure, all of the liquid will evaporate
  • If the vapor pressure of the liquid is greater than the total ambient pressure, it evaporates rapidly; it boils
    • The boiling point of a liquid is the temperature at which its vapor pressure equals the ambient pressure
  • At the boiling point, liquid and gas phases are in equilibrium
  • If a substance is in a closed container at the boiling point, the liquid boils and the gas condenses at the same rate
    • The net change in their amounts is zero
  • During a phase transition, heat transfer doesn’t cause any temperature change
  • The temperature remains constant, since the added heat is used to break the bonds between the molecules in the solid/liquid
  • Conversely, work is done by attractive forces when molecules are brought together during freezing and condensation
    • That energy must be transferred out of the system, usually as heat
    • This is why condensation occurs of cold objects like glasses
  • The energy involved in a phase change depends on the number of bonds, which is proportional to the mass of the sample
    • The energy per unit mass needed to change a substance from solid to liquid or released during the transition from liquid to solid is the heat of fusion
    • The energy per unit mass needed to change a substance from liquid to vapor is called the heat of vaporization
      • Where LfL_fLf​﻿ is the empirically determined latent heat of fusion
    • Where LvL_vLv​﻿ is the empirically defined latent heat of vaporization
    • Where LsL_sLs​﻿ is the empirically defined latent heat of sublimation
  • Latent heats are also called latent heat coefficients and heats of transformation
  • Problem-solving strategy for the effects of heat transfer
    • Determine if the system has a phase or temperature change
    • Identify all objects that change temperature or phase
    • Identify the unknowns
    • Identity the knowns
      • During a temperature change, the transferred heat depends on the specific heat of the material
      • During a phase change, the transferred heat depends on the latent heat of the substance
    • Solve the appropriate equation
      • May require multiple steps if there’s a phase change and a temperature change
      • A calorimetry problem only requires a single equation
    • Check for reasonability and consistency

Mechanisms of Heat Transfer

  • There are 3 forms of heat transfer:
    • Conduction
      • Heat transfer through macroscopically stationary matter by physical contact
      • Some materials conduct heat faster than others
        • A material that conducts heat fast is a good thermal conductor
        • A material that conducts heat slowly is a good thermal insulator
      • The rate of conduction is dependent on the temperature difference, cross-sectional area, distance between hot and cold (thickness)

      • The ratio d/k d/kd/k﻿ is called the R factor
        • Good insulators have a large R factor
        • The rate of conductive heat transfer is inversely proportional to the R factor
        • Used for household insulation, refrigerators, etc.
        • The R factor adds when 2 rods are joined end to end
      • Most of the best thermal conductors are also great electrical conductors, due to their free electrons that can transport thermal energy
    • Convection
      • Heat transfer through macroscopic movement of a fluid
      • Large scale flow of matter
      • 2 types of convection
        • Forced convection is due to flow that’s driven by fans, pumps, etc.
        • Free or natural convection is flow driven by buoyant forces; hot fluid rises while cold fluid sinks
      • Convection is complicated
        • Approximately proportional to temperature difference
        • Dependent on the available space for airflow
    • Radiation
      • Heat transfer by electromagnetic radiation
      • How the sun transfers heat to the earth
      • No medium is required
      • A shorter wavelength/higher frequency means a higher energy
      • Higher temperatures produce more intensity at every wavelength due to more heat being radiated
      • In the spectrum of visible light, a temperature change is accompanied by a colour change, since wavelength determines colour
      • The radiated energy as a function of wavelength depends on its intensity
      • In addition to temperature difference, the rate of radiation also depends on the object’s colour
        • Black is most effective
        • White is the least effective
      • The colour is mathematically represented by the emissivity e
        • A perfect blackbody has e =1
        • A perfect reflector would have e = 0
        • Emissivity controls both radiation and absorption
      • The rate of heat transfer by emitted radiation is governed by the Stefan-Boltzmann law of radiation
