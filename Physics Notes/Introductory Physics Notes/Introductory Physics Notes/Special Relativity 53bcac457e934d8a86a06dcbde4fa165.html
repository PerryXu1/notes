Special Relativity

  • The special theory of relativity was proposed by Albert Einstein in 1905
    • Describes how time, space, and other phenomena appear in different frames of reference that move at a constant velocity with respect to each other
    • Phenomena in different reference frames that accelerate with respect to each other is explained by his later theory of General Relativity
  • Measurements of length and time intervals are not in same in different reference frames moving relative to each other
    • Usually only significant at speeds comparable to the speed of light
  • Special relativity affect all fundamental physical theories

Relativity of Simultaneity

  • Classically, time intervals are independent of the observer
    • The elapsed time between two events should always be the same no matter the observed
  • At relativistic speeds, elapsed time depends on the motion of the observer relative to the location of the event
  • In order to know when to start and stop a stopwatch, we can use the arrival of light from the event
  • Suppose there are two observers: AAA﻿, who is midway on a train, and BBB﻿ who is standing on the train platform
    • There are two flash lamps, one at the back of the train car and one at the front
    • The train moves in the direction of the gray velocity arrow
    • According to observer BBB﻿, since the light beams reach him at the same time, he measures the distances from the light beams’ origins (which are the same), and concludes that the light pulses were emitted simultaneously
    • However, due to the motion of observer AAA﻿, the pulse from the front (right) reaches her first
      • She measures the distances, finds them equal, and concludes that the pulses were not emitted simultaneously
    • Two observers disagree on whether the two events were simultaneous
      • Both observer’s reference frames are equally valid, so they are both correct
  • Simultaneity is not absolute

Length Contraction

  • When relativistic speeds are involved, measured distances and lengths need not be the same for different observers
  • Two observers passing each other always see the same value of their relative speed
    • Even if they disagree on elapsed time, they will agree on their relative speeds
  • For instance, in the muon example, the observer on Earth will see the muon travel much further than the muon will see itself travel
    • Relativistic speeds mean that the muon lasts longer when observed from the Earth, which means it will travel further
  • The distance between two events depends on who measures it and how they move relative to it
  • The proper length L0L_0L0​﻿ is the distance between two points as measured by an observer at rest relative to both points
  • Length contraction is the decrease in the measured length of an object from its proper length when measured in a reference frame moving with respect to the object
    • Quantified as:
    • L0L_0L0​﻿ is the proper length, the length of the object in its rest frame
    • LLL﻿ is the length of the object in a frame moving with velocity vvv﻿ relative to the object
  • Length is always contracted; that is: L<L0L<L_0L<L0​﻿
  • Measurements of a transverse length must be the same in different inertial frames
    • Any length perpendicular to the direction of motion is not changed at all
  • At classical speeds, γ≈1\gamma \approx 1γ≈1﻿, so the proper length and contracted length are approximately the same
  • Length contraction is a real effect, demonstrated by particles in particle accelerators
    • Particles in the Stanford Linear Accelerator see the 3 km length of the accelerator as 0.5 metres
