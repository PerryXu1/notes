Faraday’s Law

  • Many applications of electromagnetism are related to time-varying electric or magnetic fields
    • Expressed through Faraday’s Law and through a displacement current term in Ampere’s Law
  • When there is relative motion between a coil and a magnet, an emf is produced in the coil
  • A changing current in one circuit also induces a current in a second nearby circuit
  • Faraday’s Law states that the emf ϵ\epsilonϵ﻿ induced is the negative rate of change of the magnetic flux Φm\Phi_mΦm​﻿ over time
    • Any change in the magnetic field/change in orientation of the area of the coil with respect to the magnetic field induces a voltage
  • The magnetic flux is a measurement of the amount of magnetic field that goes through a surface area
    • Proportional to the amount of magnetic field lines through a surface
    • Similar to electric flux
    • The SI unit for magnetic flux is the weber (Wb)
      • 1 Wb=1 T ⋅ m21 \text{ Wb} = 1 \text{ T } \cdot \text{ m}^21 Wb=1 T ⋅ m2﻿

  • In practical applications, there are many turns of wire, which each experience the same amount of magnetic flux

Lenz’s Law

  • Lenz’s Law states that the direction of the induced emf will drive current around a wire loop in such a way as to always oppose the change in magnetic flux that causes the emf
    • Statement on the conservation of energy
  • Faraday’s law can be used to determine the magnitude of ϵ\epsilonϵ﻿ and Lenz’s Law can be used to determine the direction
  • Problem-solving strategy for Lenz’s Law

    1. Determine the direction of the induced magnetic field B⃗\bm{\vec{B}}B﻿
      • The induced magnetic field will reinforce a decreasing magnetic flux or oppose an increasing magnetic flux
    1. Use the RHR to determine the direction of the current responsible for the induced magnetic field

Motional Emf

  • Changing magnetic flux can also be done by moving a conductor through a magnetic field or changing its orientation in the magnetic field
  • For a conducting rod of length lll﻿ perpendicular to and moving through a magnetic field BBB﻿ at velocity vvv﻿, the motionally induced emf can be derived
    • Works even when a closed circuit is not present
  • Faraday’s law holds for any change in flux caused by the motion of a conductor
    • A closed circuit/conductor rails are not needed
  • Lenz’s law also holds here
    • The conductor will generate a current that will generate a magnetic field to reduce the magnetic field opposite its direction of motion (behind it
  • Faraday’s Law holds for all flux changes
    • Whether caused by a changing magnetic field, relative motion, or both
  • The power dissipated by the resistor in the circuit with the moving conducting rod is equal to the power produced by the applied force to move the rod at a constant velocity
  • Motional emfs on Earth are not very large due to Earth’s weak magnetic field

Induced Electric Fields

  • The work done on the charges that creates the emf is due to the electric field induced in the wires
    • Can’t be batteries because batteries don’t need to be present in wires with induced current
    • Can’t be magnetic field because magnetic fields never do any work on moving charges
  • The change in magnetic flux induces an electric field that creates the emf in the wires
  • The induced emf ϵ\epsilonϵ﻿ is equal to the work done by the electric field over the entire wire path per unit charge
  • Faraday’s law can be written in terms of the induced electric field
  • For any closed path, the line integral of the electric field along the path is equal to the negative of the time rate of change of the magnetic flux through the surface enclosed by the closed path
  • There is an important distinction to be made between the electric field induced by a changing magnetic field and the electric field produced by a fixed charge distribution (electrostatic field)
    • The induced electric field is nonconservative
      • Does net work when it moves a charge over a closed path
      • Does work per unit charge ϵ\epsilonϵ﻿ when it moves charges over the path of the circuit
    • The electrostatic field is conservative
      • Does no net work when moving a charge over a closed path

Eddy Currents

  • If the motional emf can cause a current within the conductor, that current is called an eddy current
  • Eddy currents can produce significant drag, called magnetic damping
    • E.g. when a pendulum with a metal plate is passed through strong magnets, the eddy currents greatly slow down the pendulum
    • Slotted metal plates reduce the eddy currents since they can’t become as large
    • There is no magnetic damping on nonconducting bobs, as eddy currents are extremely small
    • As the bob approaches the first edge, there is an increase in magnetic flux, which produces a current, which produces a magnetic field which produces a force that slows down the bob
    • Between the edges, since the magnetic flux is constant, there are no eddy currents
    • At the second edge, there is an decrease in magnetic flux, which produces a current, which produces a magnetic field which produces a force that slows down the bob
  • Magnetic damping is used in sensitive lab balances to provide damping without friction
  • Braking is another application, although eddy currents cannot bring the motion to a complete stop
    • Braking force decreases as speed is reduced

Electric Generators and Back Emf

  • Electric generators induce an emf by rotating a coil in a magnetic field
    • Convert mechanical energy into electrical energy
    • Opposite of a motor
  • The emf induced in a generator with NNN﻿ turns, area AAA﻿ rotating at a constant angular velocity ω\omegaω﻿ in a uniform magnetic field BBB﻿ is given by
  • Can also be written in terms of the peak emf ϵ0\epsilon_0ϵ0​﻿
  • Motors and generators have the same general construction
  • When the coil of a motor is turned, magnetic flux changes through the coil, so an emf is produced
    • Consistent with Faraday’s law
  • A motor acts like a generator whenever its coil rotates due to a changing magnetic flux through the coil
    • Happens every time the motor is turned, whether by external input or by the motion of the motor itself
  • Whenever a motor does work and its shaft is turning, an emf that opposes the motor’s emf is induced, called the back emf
    • The back emf will reduce the driving emf of the motor
  • The generator output of a motor difference between the supply voltage/driving emf and the back emf
  • Back emf is proportional to the motor’s angular velocity
  • The back emf is zero when the motor is first turned on, so the motor will draw maximum emf and maximum current initially
  • As the motor turns faster, the back emf also grows, which reduces the emf across the coil and the amount of current it draws
    • This means a motor will initially draw more current than when it runs at its normal speed
  • If a motor runs at too low of a speed, the larger current can overheat and potential burn it out
  • If there is no load on the motor and the motor overspins, the back emf will nearly equal the driving emf, wasting a lot of energy
  • Iron cores of motors are usually constructed out of thin electrically insulated sheets of iron to prevent eddy currents
