Electromagnetic Waves

  • Light is a form of an electromagnetic wave
  • Electromagnetic waves are useful in many different ways
    • Allows us to see
    • X-rays
    • Cell phones
  • Electromagnetic waves were predicted before anyone realized that light is a form of an electromagnetic wave
  • In the mid-19th century, James Clerk Maxwell formulated a single theory of electromagnetism, summed up by four equations known as Maxwell’s Equations

Energy Carried by Electromagnetic Waves

  • Just like all other waves, the waves carry energy away from their source
    • Used in microwaves to heat up food
  • EM waves bring energy into a system through their fields, which do work on charges
  • There is also energy in the wave itself
    • If some energy of the wave is absorbed, the field strengths are diminished and continues propagating
  • The larger the strengths of the electric and magnetic fields, the more work they can do and the greater the energy within the wave

Momentum and Radiation Pressure

  • Electromagnetic waves can heat up an object by doing work on the particles of an object
  • Electromagnetic radiation can also exert an extremely weak pressure because electromagnetic waves have momentum
  • When an electromagnetic wave interacts with an electron damped by resistance, it exerts a force on it
    • When the electric field is positive (positive y-axis), the electron is pushed downwards since it’s negatively charged
    • When it’s pushed downwards, because it has velocity, the magnetic field acts on it to produce a force in the direction of propagation of the wave
    • When the electric and magnetic fields reverse, the electron is pushed upwards and acted on by the magnetic field to again produce a force in the direction of propagation
    • This produces a pressure
  • The Lorentz force equation and electromagnetic waves imply the existence of radiation pressure
  • An electromagnetic wave therefore carries momentum
  • An object that absorbs an electromagnetic wave experiences a force in the direction of propagation of the wave
    • The force is doubled if the radiation was instead reflected
  • The radiation pressure pradp_{rad}prad​﻿ applied on a perfectly absorbing surface is equal to the energy density of the wave
  • For a perfectly reflecting surface, the radiation pressure is doubled
  • Since energy density oscillates so fast, we often use the time-averaged radiation pressure, which is written in terms of intensity

Radio Waves

  • Electromagnetic radiation with wavelengths greater than 0.1 m0.1 \text{ m}0.1 m﻿ are called radio waves
  • Commonly used for audio communications like radios
  • Subdivided into many subranges
  • There is no lowest frequency, but extremely low frequency waves (ELF) waves are among the lowest frequencies commonly encountered and are between 3 hertz and 3 kilohertz
    • AC currents in power lines produce ELF waves
    • Can penetrate sea water
  • To encode information, the amplitude, frequency or phase of the wave is modulated
    • AM radios modulate the amplitude
      • Assigned frequency is between 540 and 1600 kilohertz
    • FM radios modulate the frequency
      • Assigned frequency is between 88 to 108 megahertz
  • Digital data can be encoded into the waves

Microwaves

  • Highest frequency electromagnetic waves that can be produced by macroscopic circuit’s currents
  • Also produced by electronic transitions
  • Roughly 10910^{9}109﻿ to 101210^{12}1012﻿
  • Occur naturally as the cosmic microwave background radiation
  • Can also be produced by thermal agitation (thermal motion of atoms and molecules)
    • All objects above absolute zero emit and absorb radiation due to thermal agitation
  • Radars are a common application of microwaves
    • Can be used to measure distances
  • Microwaves of 2.45 GHz2.45 \text{ GHz}2.45 GHz﻿ are used commonly in microwave ovens
    • Exerts torques on the polar water molecules, rotating them and causing them to heat up surrounding particles
  • Cell phones use microwaves to communicate
    • Transmit and receive them
  • WiFi uses microwaves
  • Used in RFID

Infrared Radiation

  • Produced by thermal motion
  • Used in thermal vision since they are given off by objects
  • Remotes use infrared to transmit their signals

Visible Light

  • Relatively narrow
  • Range of electromagnetic spectrum between ~400 nanometers to ~750 nanometers
  • Produced by vibrations and rotations of particles as well as electronic transitions
  • Used by humans to see
  • Used by plants to photosynthesize

Ultraviolet Light

  • Above visible light
  • Divided into 3 ranges
    • UV-A
      • 320-400 nanometers
    • UV-B
      • 290-320 nanometers
    • UV-C
      • 220-290 nanometers
  • 99% of all solar UV radiation reaching Earth’s surface is UV-A
  • Can cause sunburn and skin damage
  • Vitamin D production results from exposure to UV-B radiation
  • Used in counterfeit protection

X-Rays

  • Wavelengths of 10−810^{-8}10−8﻿ to 10−12 m10^{-12} \text{ m}10−12 m﻿
  • Higher energy
  • More penetrating than UV light
  • Used to treat cancer
  • Used to image objects opaque to visible light

Gamma Rays

  • Extremely high frequency EM radiation
  • The term “gamma rays” is generally used for electromagnetic radiation emitted by a nucleus
  • Many of the same uses as X-rays
