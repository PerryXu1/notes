Blackbody Radiation

  • All bodies emit electromagnetic radiation over a range of wavelengths
  • The higher the temperature of an object, the more energy radiated
  • When a body is heated and its temperature increases, the wavelength of the emitted radiation changes colours
    • Infrared to red, red to orange, etc.
  • As the temperature of an object rises, the object flows with colors corresponding to smaller and smaller wavelengths of the electromagnetic spectrum
  • The temperature TTT﻿ of the object emitting the radiation (the emitter), determines the wavelength at which the radiated energy is at its maximum
    • The sun radiates most strongly in a range of wavelengths about 560 nanometers (visible light)
    • Our bodies radiate mostly infrared light
  • Radiation incident on an object is partially reflected and partially absorbed
  • At thermodynamic equilibrium, the rate of absorption of radiation of an object is equal to its rate of emission
    • Any good absorber of radiation is also a good emitter of radiation
  • A perfect absorber of electromagnetic radiation, which absorbs all electromagnetic radiation incident upon it, is called a blackbody
    • Idealization, since no physical object can absorb all incident radiation
  • We can approximate a blackbody as a small hole in the wall of a sealed enclosure called a cavity radiator
    • The inside walls of the cavity radiator are rough and black so that any radiation entering through the hole would become trapped and absorbed
    • At thermodynamic equilibrium, the cavity walls absorb as much radiation as they emit
    • The radiation entering the hole is balanced by the radiation leaving it
  • We can analyze the light coming out of the hole
  • The electromagnetic radiation emitted by a blackbody is called blackbody radiation
  • The intensity of the blackbody radiation I(λ,T)I(\lambda, T)I(λ,T)﻿ is dependent on both the wavelength of the emitted radiation and the temperature TTT﻿ of the blackbody

Photoelectric Effect

  • When a metal surface is exposed to a monochromatic electromagnetic wave above a certain threshold frequency (below a certain wavelength), the metal will absorb the incident radiation and emit electrons
    • Called the photoelectric effect
    • The emitted electrons are called photoelectrons
  • We can use an experimental setup to study the photoelectric effect
    • The target material acts as the cathode
      • Emits photoelectrons
      • Called the photoelectrode
    • Photoelectrons are collected at the anode
      • Anode is kept at a higher potential than the cathode
    • The potential different between electrodes can be adjusted or reversed
    • The electrodes are in a evacuated glass tube
  • When the target material is not exposed to light, no current is registered since the circuit is not completed
  • When the target material is connected to the negative terminal of a battery and exposed to light, a current is registered
    • Called the photocurrent
  • If we reverse the potential different between the electrodes by connecting the target material with the positive terminal of the battery, we can slowly increase the voltage until the photocurrent stops flowing
    • The voltage at which the photocurrent stops is called the stopping potential
  • The photoelectric effect has 3 characteristics that cannot be explained by classical physics
    1. Lack of lag time
      • When radiation strikes the target material, electrons are emitted almost instantaneously, even at low radiation intensity
      • Classical physics predicts that for low-energy radiation, electrons would be emitted, but after a significant buildup period
    1. Intensity of incident radiation & kinetic energy of photoelectrons
      • When plotting the photocurrent against the (positive) applied potential difference, the current grows steadily until it reaches a plateau
        • Further potential increase does not change the photocurrent
      • A higher intensity produces a higher photocurrent
      • For a negative potential different, as the absolute value of the potential increases, the photocurrent decreases and becomes zero at the stopping potential
      • The stopping potential is independent of radiation intensity
      • A photoelectron that leaves the surface has kinetic energy KKK﻿
      • We can measure the maximum kinetic energy KmaxK_{max}Kmax​﻿ by measuring the stopping potential ΔVs\Delta V_sΔVs​﻿
        • eee﻿ is the electron charge
      • This result is independent of light intensity, which contradicts classical physics
        • In classical physics, the photoelectron can absorb energy continuously, so a high intensity should result in a high kinetic energy and vice versa
    1. The existence of a cut-off frequency
      • For any metal surface, there is a minimum frequency of radiation below which photocurrent does not occur
      • The cut-off frequency’s value depends on the physical properties of the metal
      • Kinetic energies of the photoelectrons at the surface grow linearly with increasing frequency of radiation
      • The slope of kinetic energy to frequency has one slope no matter the metal
      • In classical physics, electrons receive energy from the incident radiation continuously, so the amount of energy they receive is determined purely by the intensity of the incident light
        • There should be no cut-off frequency
        • The kinetic energy of the photoelectrons should not depend on the frequency of incident radiation
  • The photoelectric effect was explained in 1905 by Albert Einstein
  • Einstein postulated that an electromagnetic wave carries its energy in discrete packets
    • Uses Planck’s hypothesis that energy in oscillators is quantized
    • Goes beyond Planck; light itself is made out of energy quanta
  • Electromagnetic waves are quantized

The Compton Effect

  • In classical theory, whenever an electromagnetic wave is scattered by atoms, the wavelength of the scattered radiation is the same as the incident radiation
  • Observations show that when X-rays are scattered off some materials (like graphite), the scattered X-rays have different wavelengths than the wavelength of the incident X-ray
    • Studied by Arthur Compton and his collaborators
    • Explained by Compton in 1923
  • Explained by Compton using Einstein’s idea of photons
    • Firmly entrenched the idea of photons in the physics scene
  • Compton’s experiment:
    • Monochromatic X-rays with certain wavelength λ\lambdaλ﻿ are incident on a sample of graphite (the target)
    • The X-rays interact with atoms in the target and emerge as scattered X-rays with wavelength λ′\lambda'λ′﻿
    • A detector behind the target can measure the intensity of radiation scattered in any angle θ\thetaθ﻿ with respect to the direction of the incident X-ray beam
      • θ\thetaθ﻿ is the scattering angle, the angle between the direction of the scattered beam and incident beam
  • We can measure the intensity and wavelength λ′\lambda'λ′﻿ of the scattered beam for a given scattering angle θ\thetaθ﻿

    • One intensity peak is at the wavelength λ\lambdaλ﻿, and the other is at some other wavelength λ′\lambda'λ′﻿
    • The two peaks are separated by Δλ\Delta \lambdaΔλ﻿, called the Compton Shift

Wave-Particle Duality

  • Wave-particle duality exists in nature
    • All massive and massless particles have both wave and particle properties
  • A particle may act like a particle or a wave
  • Electromagnetic radiation may act like a wave or a beam of particles
  • Ages old concept
    • Newton thought light was made out of particles
    • According to Christiaan Huygens, light was a wave
    • Newton’s theory was disproved by Thomas Young’s double-slit experiment
    • James Clerk Maxwell’s theory of classical electromagnetism says that light is an electromagnetic wave
    • However, Maxwell’s theory cannot explain blackbody radiation or the photoelectric effect
  • Electrons can be used in a double-slit experiment
    • Creates an interference pattern
    • Even creates an interference pattern when single electrons are shot one by one
  • No classical particle or wave theory can fully explain the observed phenomena
  • In 1928, a foundation for a new statistical theory of physics, called quantum mechanics was developed by Niels Bohr, Erwin Schrödinger, Werner Heisenberg, and Paul Dirac
  • Quantum mechanics uses statistical interpretations of particles as described by de Broglie’s matter waves
  • A wave associated with a particle carries information about probably positions of the particle and its other properties
  • A single particle is seen as a moving wave packet similar to the icon of this page
    • Since the particle is a wave packet, we cannot measure its exact position
    • The particle has uncertainty of position Δx\Delta xΔx﻿
  • The uncertainty in a particle’s position Δx\Delta xΔx﻿ is related to the uncertainty of its linear momentum Δp\Delta pΔp﻿ by Heisenberg’s uncertainty principle
    • Our perception at the quantum level is fundamentally limited
    • If we know the exact position of a body, we cannot know its momentum since the uncertainty of position becomes infinite
    • There is a limit on the precision of simultaneous measurements of position and momentum of a particle
    • Consequence of wave-particle duality
  • Wave-particle duality is exploited by many physical systems
    • Used in electron microscopy
