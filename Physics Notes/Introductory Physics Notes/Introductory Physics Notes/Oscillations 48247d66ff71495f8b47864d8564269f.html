Oscillations

Simple Harmonic Motion

  • Periodic motion is any motion that repeats itself at regular time intervals
  • The period (TTT﻿) is the amount of time needed to complete 1 oscillation
    • SI units is seconds
    • Constant in the absence of friction
  • The frequency (fff﻿) is the oscillations per unit time
    • SI units is hertz (Hz) (1s\tfrac{1}{s}s1​﻿)
  • Period and frequency are related
  • 1 Hz is 1 complete oscillation per second
  • The angular velocity, also known as the angular frequency, is 2πT\tfrac{2\pi}{T}T2π​﻿for SHM
  • Simple harmonic motion (SHM) is a type of oscillatory motion where the net force is proportional to the displacement and opposite the displacement’s direction
  • A system with oscillates with simple harmonic motion is a simple harmonic oscillator
  • A good example of SHM is an object with a mass m attached to a spring on a frictionless surface
    • Follows Hooke’s law: F=−kxF = -kxF=−kx﻿
  • The object oscillates around the equilibrium position
  • If the net force can be described by Hooke’s law and there’s no damping, there will be equal displacements on both sides of the equilibrium position
  • The maximum displacement from equilibrium is the amplitude (AAA﻿)
  • In SHM, the period and frequency are independent of amplitude
  • The mass mmm﻿ and the spring constant kkk﻿ are the only factors which affect the frequency and period
  • The larger the spring/force constant, the stiffer the object is

Energy in Simple Harmonic Motion

  • When work is done to stretch or compress the spring, energy is given to the system as potential energy
  • In a simple harmonic oscillator, energy oscillates between the kinetic energy and potential energy
    • In the case of a mass on a spring, the potential energy is elastic potential energy: U=12kx2U=\tfrac{1}{2}kx^2U=21​kx2﻿
  • The total energy of a mass on a spring is its elastic potential energy at maximum displacement
  • The kinetic energy oscillates like a sine-squared function
  • The potential energy oscillates like a cosine-squared function
  • The total energy is proportional to the amplitude squared in many kinds of oscillations
  • The potential energy graph of a simple harmonic oscillator resembles a bowl
    • When an object is put in a bowl, it goes to the equilibrium position
    • The force that points towards the equilibrium point (AKA a fixed point) is the restoring force
    • The equilibrium point is a stable equilibrium point because forces on both sides of the fixed point points towards the fixed point
    • The points where the potential energy reaches a maximum are the turning points
  • An equilibrium point is stable if the forces on both sides of it point back towards it

Damped Oscillations

  • In the real world, friction often acts to damp the motion, slowly decreasing the oscillation’s energy
    • Usually, that energy is converted to heat
  • For a system with a small damping force, the period/frequency will remain constant and nearly the same as for SHM
    • However, the amplitude will gradually decrease

  • If b<4mkb \lt \sqrt{4mk}b<4mk​﻿:
    • The system is underdamped
    • The system oscillates with its amplitude decreasing exponentially
  • If b=4mkb = \sqrt{4mk}b=4mk​﻿:
    • The system is critically damped
    • The system doesn’t oscillate and asymptotically approaches equilibrium as quick as possible
  • If b>4mkb \gt \sqrt{4mk}b>4mk​﻿:
    • The system is overdamped
    • The system doesn’t oscillate and asymptotically approaches equilibrium over a longer period of time
  • Critical damping is often desired because it returns to equilibrium quickly and doesn’t overshoot
  • A constant force applied to a critically damped system moves the system to a new equilibrium position in the shortest time possible without overshooting or oscillating around the new position

Forced Oscillations

  • The natural frequency of a system is the frequency at which it oscillates without a damping or driving force
  • Objects can be forced to oscillate
    • They oscillate most easily at their natural frequency
  • A periodic driving force puts energy into the system at a certain frequency
    • Not necessarily the natural frequency of the system
  • When the driving force’s frequency is low, the system’s amplitude doesn’t increase much
  • When the driving force’s frequency matches the system’s natural frequency, the amplitude increases every oscillation
    • This phenomenon is called resonance
  • When the driving force’s frequency is high, the system’s amplitude doesn’t increase much

  • The maximum amplitude is reached when the angular frequency reaches the natural frequency of the system (resonance)
  • A graph of the amplitude of a simple harmonic oscillator under different dampings with respect to the natural frequency shows that a SH oscillator with the least damping will have the largest amplitude
    • The smaller the damping, the less energy taken out and thus the greater the amplitude
    • However, the resonance gets more narrow as the damping gets lower
  • If a system requires a narrow resonance, it should have minimal damping
    • For instance, a radio should have minimal damping so it can resonate with a certain frequency
