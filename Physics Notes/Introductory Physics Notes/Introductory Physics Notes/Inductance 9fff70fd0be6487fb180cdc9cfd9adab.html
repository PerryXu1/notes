Inductance

  • A current flowing in a circuit produces its own magnetic field
    • A change in this current will cause a changing magnetic field, which will induce an emf
  • Inductance is a physics quantity that quantifies how “effective” a device is at inducing emf in another device

Mutual Inductance

  • When two circuits have time-varying currents are close to each other, the magnetic flux through each circuit varies due to the changing current in the other circuit
    • The emf is therefore induced in each circuit by the varying current of the other
    • This emf is called mutually induced emf
    • This phenomenon is called mutual inductance

  • Mutual capacitance is a geometric quantity, like capacitance
    • Depends on shapes and relative positions of two coils
    • Independent of the current in the coil
  • Inductance is measured in the henry (H\text HH﻿)
  • The emf induced in a circuit can be written in terms of the mutual inductance using Faraday’s law
  • The emf induced in a circuit due to mutual inductance may not be the total emfs in each coil
  • Each coil can also have an emf induced in it due to self-inductance
  • Large mutual inductance may or may not be desirable
    • Transformers should have high mutual inductance
    • Appliances like an electric clothes dryer should not, as a high mutual inductance can lead to a dangerous emf being induced on its metal case
  • Counter-winding coils is one way to cancel the magnetic field produced and reduce mutual inductance
  • Digital signal processing use counter-would coils to reduce mutual inductance

RL Circuits

  • When the circuit is closed and current starts flowing, it induces an emf in the inductor which counteracts the current flow through the circuit
    • This causes an asymptotic approach to the max current of ϵR\dfrac{\epsilon}{R}Rϵ​﻿
