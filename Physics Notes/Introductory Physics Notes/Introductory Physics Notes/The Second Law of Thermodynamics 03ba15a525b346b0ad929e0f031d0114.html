The Second Law of Thermodynamics

Reversible and Irreversible Processes

  • A process is reversible if the system and environment can be restored to the exact same initial states they were in before the process
    • Reversible processes must be quasi-static
    • A system is easily restored to its original state, but the environment is extremely difficult to restore back to its original state
      • For example, an ideal gas expanding into a vacuum can be easily restored by pushing it back with a piston and removing some heat to restore its temperature and pressure
      • However, some heat will be dumped into the environment
  • A reversible process is an ideal process and rarely happens
  • A process can be theoretically reversible but not practically reversible
    • E.g. a gas that expanded into a vacuum CAN go back into its original state, but it’s highly unlikely given the amount of particles and interactions
  • Almost all processes are irreversible
    • The system and environment cannot be restored to its previous state
    • Also known as a natural process
    • This phenomenon is called irreversibility
  • The Clausius statement of the Second Law of Thermodynamics states that Heat will never flow spontaneously from a colder object to a hotter one
    • ‘Spontaneously’ means that there’s no third party involved

Refrigerators and Heat Pumps

  • For reversible heat engines, we can reverse them to make either a refrigerator or a heat pump, depending on the focus
    • A refrigerator removes heat from an area
    • A heat pump dumps heat to an area
    • Schematic for a refrigerator/heat pump
  • A refrigerator absorbs heat QcQ_cQc​﻿ from the cold reservoir at TcT_cTc​﻿ and discards heat QhQ_hQh​﻿ to the hot reservoir at ThT_hTh​﻿, while work WWW﻿ is done on the engine’s working substance
  • The coefficient of performance of a refrigerator (KRK_RKR​﻿) is the ratio of the heat removed to the work done by the working substance
  • The coefficient of performance of a heat pump (KPK_PKP​﻿) is the ratio of the heat added to the work done by the working substance

Statements of the Second Law of Thermodynamics

  • The Kelvin statement of the Second Law of Thermodynamics states that it is impossible to convert the heat from a single source into work without any other effect
    • Describes an unattainable “perfect engine”
    • ‘Without any other effect’ is a very strong restriction
      • If the engine doesn’t complete a cycle, the substance in the engine is not in its original state and an “other effect” has occurred
  • The Kelvin statement is equivalent to the Clausius statement if the 2 objects in the Clausius statement are cold and hot reservoirs that make a refrigerator
    • Prevents a “perfect refrigerator” that transfers heat from the cold reservoir to the hot reservoir without any external work
  • Any reversible heat engine operating between 2 reservoirs has a greater efficiency than any irreversible engine operating between those 2 same reservoirs
    • Given any 2 heat reservoirs, a reversible heat engine will always be more efficient than an irreversible engine
  • All reversible engines operating between the same 2 heat reservoirs have the same efficiency

Entropy on a Microscopic Scale

  • Entropy is related to the disorder of a system
    • An ordered deck of cards has a lower entropy than a shuffled deck of cards
  • The second law can also be stated as: in any irreversible process, the universe becomes more disordered
    • For instance, the irreversible free expansion of an ideal gas results in a larger volume, which creates more possible arrangements for the same number of atoms
    • This results in a more disordered universe
  • The Third Law of Thermodynamics states that a system cannot reach absolute zero
    • For any given physical system, T>0T > 0T>0﻿
    • As the temperature gets closer and closer to absolute zero, the change in entropy in a system approaches zero
  • As the temperature of a system approaches absolute zero, all isotherms become isentropic
    • Isentropic means constant entropy
    • All isotherms become a reversible ideal adiabat
    • A system becomes perfectly ordered when its temperature approaches absolute zero and its entropy approaches its absolute minimum
