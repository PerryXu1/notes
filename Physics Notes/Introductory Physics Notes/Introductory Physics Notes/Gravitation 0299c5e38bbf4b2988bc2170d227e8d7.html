Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation

  • History of gravitation
    • Aristotle thought that rocks would seek Earth and fire would seek the Heavens because of their natures
    • Brahmagupta hypothesized that the Earth was a sphere and objects possessed a natural affinity for it
    • Ptolemy studied celestial motions, but failed to connect them to the motion of bodies on Earth
    • Nicolaus Copernicus was the first person to suggest a heliocentric model
      • Supported by the work of Tycho Brahe, Johannes Kepler and Galileo Galilei
    • Kepler showed that the motion of each planet is an ellipse
    • Robert Hooke suggested that those motions were due to attraction to the sun
    • Sir Isaac Newton finally connected the acceleration of objects on Earth to centripetal acceleration of the Moon about Earth
    • In 1916, Albert Einstein proposed his theory of General Relativity, the most accurate model of gravity
  • Newton’s law of universal gravitation states that the force of gravitation between 2 objects is proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to distance between them
    • FG,AB⃗\vec{F_{G,AB}}FG,AB​​﻿ is the force that object B exerts on object A and rABr_{AB}rAB​﻿ is a unit vector points from object A to object B
    • GGG﻿ is the universal gravitational constant, mmm﻿ is the mass of the object, and rrr﻿ is the distance between them
  • The Cavendish experiment found the value of the universal gravitational constant
  • Problem solving strategy for Newton’s law of universal gravitation:
    • Identify the masses in play
    • Draw a free-body diagram
    • Apply Newton’s law of universal gravitation and second law of motion

Tidal Forces

  • Ocean tides are due to gravitational tidal forces
    • Combination of the Earth’s rotation about its axis and gravitational attraction to the Moon and Sun
  • Moon’s effect on tides
    • One side of the Earth is closer to the Moon than the other
    • This causes a high tide (tidal bulge) on the farthest and nearest side of the Earth to the Moon
      • On the near side, the water is pulled more by the Moon
      • On the far side, the water bulges because the Moon pulls the Earth more than it
    • The tidal bulge also creates low tides on the other sides of the Earth
    • The Earth thus (in isolation) orbits about the Earth-Moon center of mass
      • About 1600 km below the Earth’s surface and along the line between the Earth and Moon
    • The tidal force is the difference between the force at the center of the Earth and at any other point
      • Difference in gravitational forces between the farthest and nearest side
    • Relative to the moon, the bulges stay fixed along the line between the Earth and Moon
    • Tides rise and fall twice per day because the Earth rotates
      • In 6 hours, the near and far locations of the Earth move to where the low tides were
      • After another 6 hours, the positions move back
      • Due to the orbital period (28 days) of the moon, the time between high tides is ~12.5 hours
  • Sun’s effect on tides
    • The Sun exerts a tidal force
      • Its gravitational attraction on earthbound objects is around 200 times that of the moon
    • However, the Sun’s tidal effect is less than that of the Moon
    • Relative positions of the Sun and the Moon can create the smallest tides (neap tides) and the largest tides (spring tides)
      • Spring tides occur when the Moon, Sun and Earth are on the same line
        • Moon is either behind Earth or in front
      • Neap tides occur when the Moon, Sun and Earth form a right-angled triangle
        • Gravitational pulls on the Earth are perpendicular
  • The magnitude of the tides is very complicated
    • Tidal forces get stronger as distances become smaller
      • Tides are very large when the Earth is at perihelion and the Moon is at perigee
      • Tides are very small when the Earth is at aphelion and the Moon is at apogee
    • Tidal magnitude is the most dependent on the topography of the shoreline and the bathymetry (profile of depth)
  • Tidal forces are causing the Earth’s rotation rate to slow down
    • Tidal forces convert the Earth’s rotational energy to heat
  • Tidal forces also cause locking, the dissipation of a body’s angular momentum
    • One side of the body will always be pointing towards the larger mass
    • The Moon is locked towards Earth
    • Eventually, the Earth will be locked towards the Moon, although it will be many billions of years
  • Tidal forces can rip matter from stars

Einstein’s Theory of Gravity

  • In 1905, Albert Einstein proposed his theory of Special Relativity
    • This introduced a speed limit on the universe, the speed of light in a vacuum
  • The speed limit challenged Newton’s law of gravitation, which assumed that gravity was an action-at-a-distance-force
    • Newton assumed that, without physical contact, any change in the position of a mass is instantly communicated to all other masses
  • In 1916, Albert Einstein introduced his theory of General Relativity
    • He proposed a theory of space-time geometry
    • How mass and acceleration distort and interact with space-time
  • In general relativity, there is no difference between free fall and weightlessness
    • Called the principle of equivalence
    • Thus, there is no difference between a uniform gravitational field and uniform acceleration without gravity
    • Therefore, any physical experiments done on Earth (essentially uniform g field) should have the same results as ones done on a spaceship accelerating at a = g
  • General relativity is predicated on non-Euclidean geometry
    • In Euclidean geometry, parallel lines never meet and the sum of angles of a triangle is always 180
  • Only empty space is flat
  • Mass/energy distorts and curves the space-time around it
  • The motion of mass is due to the curved space-time
  • An orbit is thus the satellite following the curved spacetime produced by the larger mass
    • Note: the satellite would distort space-time, and this would be in 3 dimensional
  • For weak gravitational fields, Newton’s law of gravitation is an extremely good approximation
  • However, the results of the 2 theories diverge for intense gravitational fields, and general relativity has predicted the correct results
  • For instance, the precession of Mercury’s orbit
    • Mercury has an elliptical orbit, but its perihelion slowly advances
    • This cannot be explained by Newton’s law, but is explained by general relativity
  • When space-time is distorted, the nearby space is ‘stretched out’ and the time is dilated/’slowed’
  • General relativity is expressed in a tensor equation, the Einstein Field Equation
    • Tensors are generalisations of scalars and vectors
  • One of the solutions of the equation predicts a black hole
    • A black hole is an extremely dense object, bounded by an event horizon, from which nothing, not even light, can escape
    • The name ‘black hole’ was coined by astronomer John Wheeler, in 1969
    • The first person to notice the phenomenon was Karl Schwarzchild, in 1916
  • An object with mass MMM﻿ will turn into a black hole when compressed to its Schwarzschild radius
    • Substituting ccc﻿ into the escape velocity equation yields this, and while it is correct, it is a coincidence
    • The correct answer, found using Einstein's field equations, is the same
  • The Schwarzschild radius is also called the event horizon
  • If a neutron star gained enough additional mass, it would collapse and shrink beyond the Schwarzschild radius into a singularity
  • At a singularity, space-time is stretched infinitely
    • As outside observers, an object would slow to a halt at the event horizon, since time is dilated to infinity
    • Note: not to scale
  • When we estimate the mass of stars in our galaxy and the mass of the black hole at the center of our galaxy, we get a discrepancy
    • The orbital speed of the stars is too fast for that much matter
    • This reveals dark matter, the matter we don’t see that causes this discrepancy
    • There’s 5 times as much dark matter as visible matter
    • There’s also a discrepancy in the distribution of matter
  • This phenomenon is a feature of all galaxies
  • The orbit velocity of stars in the Milky Way as a function of radius
  • There are 2 main theories of dark matter: WIMPs and MACHOs
    • WIMPs: weakly interacting massive particles
    • MACHOs: massive compact halo objects
