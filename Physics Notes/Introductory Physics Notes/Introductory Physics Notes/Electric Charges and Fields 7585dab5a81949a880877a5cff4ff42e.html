Electric Charges and Fields

Electric Charge

  • The electric force acts on all objects with a property called charge
    • Much stronger than gravity
    • Helps keep atoms together
  • Static electricity causes many things, such as paper or clothes, to stick to each other
    • Displays an electric property
  • That electric property, charge, comes in 2 types: positive and negative
    • The electric force between 2 objects with the same type of charge is repulsive
      • Electrostatic repulsion
    • The electric force between 2 objects with opposite types of charge attract
      • Electrostatic attraction
  • The SI unit of electric charge is the coulomb (C)
  • Along with gravity, the electric force is a long range/at a distance force
  • As distance between the 2 charged objects increase, the force between them rapidly decreases
    • Inverse-square law
  • Charge is quantized
    • There is a smallest possible amount of charge
      • That smallest amount is e=1.602×10−19 Ce = 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}e=1.602×10−19 C﻿
    • The charge on any object must be an integer multiple of the smallest possible amount
    • All macroscopic charged objects have charge because electrons have either been added or taken away, which results in a net charge
  • The charge magnitude is independent of the type
    • The magnitudes of the smallest positive charge and the smallest negative charge are the same
      • +1.602×10−19 C vs. −1.602×10−19 C+1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C vs. } -1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}+1.602×10−19 C vs. −1.602×10−19 C﻿
  • Charge is conserved
    • Charge is neither created nor destroyed; it can only be transferred between objects
    • Charges cancelling out means that the amount of charges of each type are the same, not that the charges are physically destroyed
    • The net charge of the universe is constant
  • Charge is conserved in closed systems
    • If the total charge of a local system is changing, there will be a measurable flow of charge in/out of the system
  • Charge is conserved and Charge is conserved in closed systems is known as the law of conservation of charge
  • Negatively charged particles in the atom are electrons
    • 1837 times smaller than a proton/neutron
  • Positively charged particles in the atom are protons
  • Neutral particles in the atom are neutrons
    • Roughly the same mass as the proton
  • The Bohr model of the atom displays the atom as electrons in quantized orbits orbiting a nucleus consisting of protons and neutrons
  • An atom with a charge is called an ion

Conductors, Insulators, and Charging by Induction

  • The valence electrons of an atom can become free or conduction electrons when electricity flows
  • A material with a large amount of conduction electrons is good at conducting electricity and is called a conductor
    • If excess charge is added to a conductor, it becomes spread evenly throughout the surface of the conductor
  • A material that lacks conduction electrons is bad at conducting electricity is called an insulator
    • If excess charge is added to an insulator, it stays in place
  • Charging by induction
    • Charging by induction utilities another charged object to polarize an object
      • Polarisation is the separating of positive and negative charges
    • The resulting polarisation forms an electric dipole
    • The dipole can be used in order to permanently charge objects
  • Neutral objects can be attracted to any charged object due to polarisation
  • Ground is any object that can act as a sink for excess electrons or supply charge

  • A continuous charge distribution has at least one nonzero dimension and can be divided into infinitesimal pieces
  • Calculating the electric fields of continuous charge distributions is much more difficult

Electric Field Lines

  • Electric field lines are lines that can be used to visualize an electric field
    • Small vector arrows are connected to form continuous lines
  • At every point, the field vector is tangent to the field line at that same point
  • The magnitude of the field is indicated by the field line density
    • Amount of field lines per unit area passing through a small cross-sectional area perpendicular to the electric field
    • Given the same number of field lines, if the field lines are closer together at a cross-sectional area, that area has a stronger field
  • Problem-solving strategy for drawing electric field lines
    • Electric field lines either originate from positive charges or come in from infinity, and either terminate on negative charges or extend out to infinity
    • The number of field lines originating/terminating on a charge is linearly proportional to the magnitude of the charge
      • A charge of 2q has twice as much field lines as a charge of q
    • At every point in space, the field vector at that point is parallel to the field line at that point
    • The field line density at any point in space is proportional to the magnitude of the field at that point in space
    • Field lines can never cross

Electric Dipoles

  • A dipole consists of 2 equal and opposite charges that are close to each other
    • Distance ddd﻿ between the charges is much much less than the position PPP﻿ that the field is being calculated at
  • A permanent dipole exists without an external field and doesn’t break apart in the external field
  • An external electric field causes the dipole to rotate
    • Net force is zero, but the net torque is non-zero
    • Rotation causes dipole to align with the external electric field
  • The dipole moment is the vector distance between the charges scaled by the magnitude pf each charge
  • The torque created by external electric field can be written in terms of the dipole moment
  • Neutral atoms, since they are neutral and spherically symmetrical, they don’t have a built-in dipole moment
    • However, when placed in an external magnetic field, they obtain one due to opposite forces acting on the positive nucleus and the negative electrons
    • This creates an induced dipole moment
  • An induced dipole moment is aligned parallel to the external electric field
    • Generally, an induced dipole moment is much smaller than an inherent dipole moment

    • Crucial for capacitors
  • The field of a dipole can be written in terms of the dipole moment
