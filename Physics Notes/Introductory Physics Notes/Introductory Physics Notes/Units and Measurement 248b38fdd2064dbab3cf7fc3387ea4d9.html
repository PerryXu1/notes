Units and Measurement

Units and Standards

  • Physical quantities are properties of objects that can be measured or calculated from other units
  • Units are standards for measuring physical quantities
  • All units can be expressed as a combination of 4 fundamental units
  • The standard units are the metre (length), kilogram (mass), second (time) and ampere (electric current)

Estimates and Fermi Calculations

  • Estimates can be used to develop intuition and check answers
  • Estimates are guesses using prior experience and logical reasoning
  • Some strategies for estimation
    • Breaking down lengths: break down large lengths into smaller lengths, estimate the smaller lengths, and multiply to get the larger lengths
      • For example, to estimate the height of a building, break it down into separate floors, and then further break it down into the height of a person, find that, and then multiply back up
    • Breaking down areas: for a complex shape, you can break it down into simple shapes like spheres, prisms, rectangles, and then estimate the lengths and areas with formulas
    • Get masses from volumes and densities: estimate volume and density, and then use them to find mass
    • Bound the quantity: bound the dimensions of the quantity with smaller and larger objects; the tighter the bounds the better
      • For example, a car is smaller than a pickup truck and larger than a Volkswagen beetle
    • One significant figure is fine: for an estimate, there isn’t any need to go beyond one significant figure; keep the arithmetic simple
    • Is it reasonable?: Is the answer reasonable?

    Significant Figures

    • Accuracy of a measured value is how close it is to the true value
    • Precision of a measured value is how close the value is to other measured values
    • Significant figures express the precision of a measuring tool
    • When multiplying/dividing measured values, the answer can contain only as many significant figures as the least precise value
      • Example: the area of a circle with radius 1.2m is expressed as 4.5m2 because 1.2 has 2 significant digits
    • When adding/subtracting values, the final answer cannot contain more decimal places than the least precise value

    • Uncertainty is usually given as a value plus or minus another value

    • When quantities are multiplied/divided the percent uncertainties are summed
