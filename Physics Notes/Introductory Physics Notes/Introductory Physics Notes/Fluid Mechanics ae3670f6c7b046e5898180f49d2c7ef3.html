Fluid Mechanics

Fluids, Density, and Pressure

  • Matter has 3 common states of matter: solid, liquid, gas
  • Solids
    • Rigid
    • Have specific shapes and definite volumes
    • Atoms/molecules are close to each other, with a significant force between them
    • A large force is required to deform a solid
    • Sometimes, molecules are organised into lattices, modelled by molecules connected by stiff springs
    • Resists shearing forces
  • Fluids
    • Fluids constantly deform to shearing forces
      • Solids resist them
    • Liquids
      • Molecules are bonded to neighbouring molecules, but have less bonds
      • Molecules are pretty close
      • Molecules can move with respect with each other
        • Molecules are not locked in place
      • Definite volume, but takes the shape of its container
      • Deform easily
      • Does not spring back to original shape
      • Essentially incompressible
    • Gases
      • Not bonded to neighbouring atoms
      • No specific shape or definite volume
      • Move to fill the container they’re in
      • molecules/atoms are far away from each other
  • Density is the mass per unit volume
  • The SI unit of density is kgm3\dfrac{kg}{m^3}m3kg​﻿
  • The CGS unit of density is gcm3\dfrac{g}{cm^3}cm3g​﻿
    • 1gcm3=1000kgm31 \dfrac{g}{cm^3} = 1000 \dfrac{kg}{m^3}1cm3g​=1000m3kg​﻿
  • Density is useful for determining if an object sinks or floats in a fluid
  • The densities of solids and liquids are roughly the same, because their atoms are in close contact
    • Gases are much less dense than solids and liquids
  • The density of solids and liquids usually increases as temperature decreases
  • Density is not always constant through an object
    • A substance with constant density throughout has homogeneous density
    • A substance with variable density throughout has heterogeneous density
    • The density at a specific location in a object with heterogeneous density is the local density
      • The local density can be obtained by taking the limit of the density of a volume as the volume approaches zero
  • The specific gravity of a material is the ratio of its density to the density of water at 4.0 degrees Celsius and under 1 atmosphere of pressure (1 g/cm3 or 1000 kg/m3)
    • Dimensionless
  • Pressure is the perpendicular force per unit area that the force is applied on
  • The pressure at a point is the infinitesimal element of force over the infinitesimal element of area
  • Although force is a vector, pressure is a scalar
    • Proportional to the magnitude of the force acting perpendicular to the area
  • The SI unit of pressure is the Pascal (Pa), named after physicist Blaise Pascal
  • A shearing force applied on a fluid will cause the fluid molecules to flow to accommodate it
  • A perpendicular force to a fluid will compress or expand the fluid
  • When a fluid is compressed, it will produce a reaction force at each point inside the fluid, which points outwards

      • Otherwise there would be a net force
    • The pressure at a point in a static fluid is dependent only on the depth
  • The density of a fluid increases as depth increases due to the weight of the higher fluid
  • The pressure in a fluid depends on the height, not the shape

  • Gauge pressure is the pressure relative to the atmospheric pressure

  • Absolute pressure is the total pressure, which includes atmospheric pressure

Pascal’s Principle and Hydraulics

  • Pascal’s principle states that when a change in pressure is applied to an enclosed fluid, it is transmitted undiminished to all portions of the fluid and to the walls of its container
  • Hydraulic systems are based off Pascal’s principle
    • Hydraulic systems can change the ratios of areas to increase or decrease forces
  • A hydraulic jack uses Pascal’s principle to lift heavy loads like cars
  • Where F1F_1F1​﻿ is the force applied and F2F_2F2​﻿ is the weight of the car
  • A hydraulic system can increase force, but cannot do more work than is done on it
    • If energy isn’t lost, the work done by each area will be equal
    • If a force is magnified tenfold, the distance it moves will be ten times less

Archimedes’ Principle and Buoyancy

  • Since pressure increases with depth, the upward force is larger than the downward force
  • The net upwards force is called the buoyant force, and acts on any object in any fluid
  • Archimedes’ principle states that the buoyant force on an object is equal to the weight of the fluid it displaces
    • Works for objects submerged in a fluid, whether partially or wholly
  • A boat floats while a rock sinks because the boat displaces more water and thus experiences a greater buoyant force
  • The fraction of submersion of an object’s volume is the ratio of its density to the fluid’s density
  • Apparent weight is an object’s measured weight when submerged in a fluid
    • Apparent weight loss is equal the weight of the fluid displaced
    • Apparent mass loss is equal to the mass of fluid displaced

Fluid Dynamics

  • Fluid dynamics is the study of fluids in motion
    • Can be extremely complex
  • An ideal fluid is a fluid with negligible viscosity
  • Viscosity is a measure of the internal friction in a fluid
  • An incompressible fluid requires an extremely large force to change its volume, and this has homogeneous density
  • Fluid motion can be represented by velocity vectors or by streamlines
    • Velocity vectors are often used in meteorology
    • Streamlines represent the path of a small volume of fluid as it flows
      • The velocity is tangential to the streamline
  • Laminar flow is represented by parallel streamlines
    • Note: the fluid's velocity decreases towards the walls due the viscosity of the fluid and the friction of the pipe walls
  • Turbulent flow is represented by irregular and always-changing streamlines
    • This can occur when the fluid reaches a critical speed
  • The volume flow rate (QQQ﻿), also known as the flow rate, is the volume of fluid flowing through an area during a period of time
  • The SI unit of flow rate is m3/sm^3/sm3/s﻿

  • Since liquids are essentially incompressible, the equation of continuity is valid for all liquids
  • Gases are compressible, so the equation must be applied with caution
  • The rate of flow of a fluid can also be described by the mass flow rate

Viscosity and Turbulence

  • Viscosity is fluid friction, both within in the fluid and between the fluid and its surroundings
    • For instance, water has low viscosity, whereas molasses has high viscosity
  • Laminar flow is flow where the layers of the fluid flow without mixing
    • Viscosity causes drag between layers, as well as the between the fluid and the surface, so the top layer, which is furthest away moves the fastest
  • Turbulent flow is fluid flow where the layers mix together with eddies and swirls
    • Can be caused by obstructions or sharp corners, as they impart velocities perpendicular to the flow
    • Can also be caused by high speeds, due to the drag between fluid layers and the fluid and its surroundings
  • Cohesive forces are forces between the fluid and itself
  • Adhesive forces are forces between the fluid and external objects
