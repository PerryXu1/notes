Capacitors and Capacitance

  • Capacitors are important electronic components
  • A capacitor is a device used to store electrical charge and electrical energy
  • Two electrical conductors separated by a fixed distance
    • Conductors are called electrodes/capacitor plates
  • The space between capacitors may be a vacuum, but it’s often filled with an insulating material called a dielectric
  • Capacitance is a property that describes the amount of storage in a capacitor
  • A capacitor made of 2 identical parallel conducting plates is called a parallel plate capacitor
  • The capacitance CCC﻿ of a capacitor is the charge stored by a capacitor per unit volt
  • The SI unit of capacitance is the farad (F) named after Michael Faraday
    • 1 Farad is one coulomb per volt
  • Since 1 C is a large amount of charge, 1 farad is a very large capacitance in a real-world application
    • In real-world applications, capacitance varies from picofarads to millifarads
  • Problem-solving strategy for calculating capacitance
    1. Assume the capacitor has charge Q
    1. Determine the electric field E⃗\bm{\vec{E}}E﻿ between the conductors
      • Gauss’s law may be used if there is symmetry present
    1. Calculate the voltage difference between the two plates by integrating the electric field from one plate to the other
    1. Plug in the voltage into the capacitance formula to obtain the capacitance
  • For any capacitor with a vacuum between it, the capacitance is determined by the geometry
  • A spherical capacitor is a capacitor made out of two concentric conducting spheres
    • The capacitance of a spherical capacitor is also geometrically dependent and can be determined with Gauss’s Law
  • A capacitor made out of a single isolated conducting sphere can be imagined as a spherical capacitor with the second sphere having an infinite radius
  • A cylindrical capacitor is a capacitor made out of two concentric conducting cylinders
  • Cylindrical capacitance is useful for coaxial cables
    • 2 concentric cylindrical conductors separated by an insulator
    • An important quantity in coaxial cables is their capacitance per unit length
  • Common insulators between the two conductors include mica, ceramic, paper, and Teflon
  • An electrolytic capacitor is a common capacitor type and uses an oxidized metal in a conducting paste
    • Has a relatively high capacitance (Can be up to 1F)
    • Only works correctly if the metal is at a higher potential than the conducting paste
    • Electrolytic action destroys the oxide film if the polarity is reversed
    • Does not work with AC circuits as the polarity would be wrong half the time
  • A variable air capacitor has 2 sets of parallel plates, one of which is fixed and one of which is variable and can be rotated
    • The fixed one is called the stator
    • The variable one is called the rotor
    • By rotating, the overlapping cross-sectional area can be changed, allowing for capacitance to be tuned to a specific values

Capacitors in Series and in Parallel

  • Circuit representations of capacitors
    1. Common capacitor
    1. Electrolytic capacitor
    1. Variable-capacitance capacitor
  • In series, equivalent capacitance is the reciprocal of the sum of the reciprocals of each individual capacitor
  • In a series network of capacitors, the equivalent capacitance will always be smaller than the smallest individual capacitance in the network
  • In parallel, the equivalent capacitance is the sum of each component’s capacitance
  • In a parallel network of capacitors, the equivalent capacitance will always be larger than the largest individual capacitance in the network

Energy Stored in a Capacitor

  • Capacitors store energy in the form of electrostatic potential energy
    • Related to both the voltage and charge stored
  • Stores energy in the electrical field between the plates
  • When a capacitor is being charged, its electric field builds up
  • When a charged capacitor is disconnected from a battery, it remains charged and thus it still stores energy
  • Energy density is the energy per unit volume
  • In a parallel plate capacitor with volume AdAdAd﻿, its energy density is dependent only on the electric field’s magnitude
  • The energy stored in a capacitor can be written in terms of the voltage, charge, and capacitance

Capacitor with a Dielectric

  • A dielectric is an insulating material that is placed between the plates of a capacitor
  • The dielectric affects the capacitance of the capacitor
  • When a dielectric is placed between the plates, the voltage is reduced by a factor κ\kappaκ﻿
    • κ\kappaκ﻿ is the dielectric constant, and κ>1\kappa \gt 1κ>1﻿
  • The value is determined empirically and is characteristic for the material
  • The dielectric has no effect on the charge stored on each plate
  • The capacitance is multiplied by the dielectric constant when a dielectric is used
  • Capacitance of certain capacitors can now be rewritten
  • The dielectric constant of a vacuum is 1
  • When a dielectric is used, the potential energy stored in the capacitor is reduced by the dielectric constant
  • The excess energy is spent polarizing the electrons in the dielectric material

Molecular Model of a Dielectric

  • All molecules can be classified as either polar or nonpolar
  • A polar molecule has a net separation of positive and negative charges, whereas a nonpolar molecule does not
  • Polar molecules have dipole moments and nonpolar molecules do not
  • Nonpolar molecules can become polar in the presence of an external electric field, in induced polarization
  • In a dielectric with polar molecules, the electric dipoles are randomly oriented
  • Under an external electric field, the polar molecules orient themselves with the field
  • Opposite charges on neighbouring charges in the center will cancel out, leaving no net charge within the dielectric
  • Close to the edges, the alignment of the dipoles does produce a net charge
  • As a result, the dielectric itself is neutral, but it has surface charges that are equal and opposite
  • The induced surface charges +Qi+Q_i+Qi​﻿ and −Qi-Q_i−Qi​﻿ produces an additional electric field, Ei⃗\bm{\vec{E_i}}Ei​​﻿ (induced electric field), and opposes the external field
  • In dielectrics with nonpolar molecules, the same effect happens since each molecule acquires an induced dipole moment from the electric field
  • In both cases, the dipoles/induced dipoles align with the field to create an opposing electric field within the capacitor
  • The total electric field can be determined by taking the vector sum of the electric field due to the free charge Q0Q_0Q0​﻿ and the electric field due to the induced charge QiQ_iQi​﻿
  • The net field can be considered to be the field produced by an effective charge Q0−QiQ_0 - Q_iQ0​−Qi​﻿
  • The dielectric constant is the ratio of the electric field due to the free charge to the total net electric field
  • EEE﻿ is always less than E0E_0E0​﻿, so κ>1\kappa \gt 1κ>1﻿
  • The induced electric field can be written in terms of the dielectric constant and the electric field due to free charge
  • When the magnitude of the external electric field becomes too large, the molecules of the dielectric start becoming ionized
    • Electrons are removed from the atom/molecule and become free electrons
    • This allows the dielectric to conduct, allowing charge to move between the plates
    • Capacitor will no longer work
  • This phenomenon is called dielectric breakdown
