Scalars and Vectors

  • Scalar: a quantity that does not have directions attached to it; only magnitude
    • Examples: time, mass, energy, temperature, speed, distance
  • Vector: a quantity that does have directions attached to it; direction and magnitude
    • Examples: displacement, force, torque, position, velocity
  • Vectors can be added, subtracted or multiplied
  • Vectors are drawn as arrows with length representing magnitude
  • Vectors are defined with a arrow above the variable that represents it
    • Displacement: d⃗\vec{d}d﻿
  • The absolute value of a vector is its magnitude; ∣a∣=a|a| = a∣a∣=a﻿
  • 2 vectors that have the same magnitude but opposite direction are antiparallel; vectors are negative of each other
  • 2 vectors with identical directions are said to be parallel
  • 2 vectors are equal if they are parallel and have equal magnitudes
  • 2 vectors are orthogonal if they are perpendicular to each other
  • Vectors can be multiplied by scalars, added to other vectors, or subtracted from other vectors
  • In a vector equation, both sides of the equation must be vectors
  • When a vector is multiplied by a scalar, its magnitude is multiplied by that scalar
    • In general, a scaled vector is parallel to the original vector
    • When the scalar is negative, the direction of the scaled vector is antiparallel to the original vector
  • The sum of 2 or more vectors is a resultant vector
    • Vector addition can be demonstrated graphically by lining up the vectors head to tail and drawing a vector from the tail of the first vector to the head of the last vector
    • Computationally, you add up the x values and the y values
    • Vector subtraction can be demonstrated graphically by making the “negative” vector antiparallel to the original vector and then subtracting them
      • A⃗−B⃗\vec{A}-\vec{B}A−B﻿ , B is made antiparallel to itself
    • Computationally, you subtract the x values and the y values
  • Vector addition is commutative and associative
  • Scalar multiplication is distributive
  • A unit vector is a unitless vector with a magnitude of 1; denoted with a letter with a hat

Algebra of Vectors

  • A vector can also be written with a length and an angle
  • The length of a vector can be found with the Pythagorean theorem
  • The angle of a vector can be found using the arctan function
  • The components of a vector can be found using trigonometric functions
  • When describing rotation, the polar coordinate system is used
  • The polar coordinate system uses an angle theta and a radius r
  • In three dimensions, i^,j^,k^\hat{i}, \hat{j}, \hat{k} i^,j^​,k^﻿ are used
  • The generalization of zero in vector algebra is the null vector; 0
    • All components of the null vector are zero
  • Thus, 2 vectors are equal if their difference is the null vector
  • To find the unit vector of direction V^\hat{V}V^﻿, for any vector V⃗\vec{V}V﻿, divide by the magnitude ∣V∣|V|∣V∣﻿

Products of Vectors

  • Vectors can be multiplied by another vector
  • Vectors cannot be divided by another vector
  • One type of multiplication is the scalar product, also known as the dot product/inner product
    • The scalar product outputs a scalar
    • Denoted by dot, A ⋅ BA \space \cdot \space BA ⋅ B﻿
    • Where θ is the angle between the vectors (direction of angle does not matter; can be measured from either vector)
    • Rearranging for theta can also be used to find the angles that vectors make with each other
    • Can also be thought of as the sum of the products of the x and y components of the vectors
    • Can also be interpreted as the product of B and the projection of A onto B or vice versa
    • The dot product of any vector with the unit vector i gives the x-component of that vector
    • The dot product of any vector with the unit vector j gives the y-component of that vector
    • The dot product of 2 parallel vectors is the product or their lengths
    • The dot product of 2 orthogonal vectors is zero
    • The dot product of 2 antiparallel vectors is the negative of the product of their lengths
    • The dot product of a vector with itself is the square of its length
    • Dot products of unit vectors with other unit vectors always equals zero
    • Dot multiplication is commutative and distributive
  • Another type of multiplication is the vector product, also known as the cross product/outer product
    • The vector product outputs a vector
    • Denoted by the cross sign
    • The cross product of vectors is a vector perpendicular to both (following the right hand rule) with magnitude ABsin⁡(θ)AB\sin(\theta)ABsin(θ)﻿
    • Where θ is the angle between the 2 vectors; measured from the first vector
      • In this case, θ is measured from A
    • A x B can also be interpreted as a vector perpendicular to both vectors (following the right hand rule) with magnitude equal to the area of the parallelogram made by the vectors (where if A is to the left of B, it’s negative and if A is to the right of B, it’s positive)
    • The cross product is the null vector for vectors that are parallel or antiparallel
    • The cross product is anticommutative
    • The maximum value of a cross product occurs when the vectors are orthogonal
    • The cross product is distributive
    • The cross products of the unit vectors are cyclic
