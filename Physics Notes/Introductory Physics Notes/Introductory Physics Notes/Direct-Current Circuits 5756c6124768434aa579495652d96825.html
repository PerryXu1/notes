Electromotive Force

  • A circuit is a collection of electrical components that performs a specific task
  • Battery output voltage decreases as the battery is depleted
  • All voltage sources have 2 parts:
    • Source of electrical energy
    • Internal resistance
  • All voltage sources create a potential difference
    • Electromotive force (emf) is a special type of potential difference
  • Batteries can be modelled as 2 terminals
    • One at a higher electric potential (positive)
    • One at a lower electric potential (negative)
  • The source of the emf of a circuit is a positive and negative terminal
  • The emf source does work on the charges to move them from the positive terminal to the negative
    • The electric field force on the negative charge is opposite the direction of the electric field, so work must be done on it
    • The energy needed to do the work comes from the chemical reactions in the battery
  • The emf is the work done on the charge per unit charge when there’s no current flowing
  • The terminal voltage VterminalV_{terminal}Vterminal​﻿ of a battery is the voltage as measured across the terminals of the battery
    • Commonly referred to as VVV﻿
  • An ideal battery maintains a constant terminal voltage equal to the emf, since it has no internal resistance
  • Internal resistance (rrr﻿) is the resistance to the flow of current within the voltage source itself
    • It generally increases as a battery is depleted
  • The terminal voltage is related to the current, emf and internal resistance
  • The terminal voltage is equal to the potential drop across a load resistor

  • Some batteries can be recharged by passing a current in the opposite direction to the current they supply
    • This causes the terminal voltage of the battery to be greater than the emf, since current is negative
  • Analysis of circuits is done with the terminal voltage
    • The internal resistance of the battery is difficult to measure and can change over time

Resistors in Series and Parallel

  • If multiple resistors are connected to a voltage source, the current supplied by the voltage source is dependent on the equivalent resistance
    • Resistors can be connected in series or in parallel
  • Series circuits
    • A circuit is in series when the current flows through the resistors sequentially
    • The current in through each resistor is the same
    • The voltage drop through each resistor varies, but the sum of voltage drops and the voltage supplied must equal zero
      • Known as Kirchhoff’s loop law
    • The equivalent resistance of resistors in series is the algebraic sum of each resistor’s resistance
    • Opposite to equivalent capacitance
    • If one component in a series circuit is affect, all other components are affected
  • Parallel circuits
    • Resistors are in parallel when one end of all resistors are connected by a continuous wire of negligible resistance
    • The voltage drop through each parallel resistor is the same
    • The current through each resistor varies, but sums up to the input current
      • Found using ohm’s law I = V/R
      • Called Kirchhoff’s junction rule
    • The equivalent resistance of resistors in parallel is the reciprocal of the sum of the reciprocals of the resistances
    • Opposite to equivalent capacitance
  • The total power dissipated by resistors equals the power supplied
  • Given any resistors, the equivalent resistance will be smaller in parallel than in series
  • More complex circuits are often combinations of series and parallel connections
    • Parts of the circuit can be reduced to equivalent resistances and further reduced until there’s a single equivalent resistance
    • Equivalent resistance is denoted as ReqR_{eq}Req​﻿
  • Problem-solving strategy for series and parallel resistors
    • Determine whether resistors are in series, parallel, or a combination of both
    • Use features of series/parallel circuits to solve for unknowns
    • Check for reasonability and consistency in the answers
  • Wire resistance is in series with other resistances that are in parallel
  • A multi-loop circuit consists of junctions, AKA nodes
    • A junction is a connection of 3+ wires

Kirchhoff’s Rules

  • Kirchhoff’s rules can be used to analyze any circuit, simple or complex
    • Kirchhoff’s junction rule:

    • The sum of all currents entering a junction must equal the sum of all currents leaving the junction
    • Holds true since current is the flow of charge and charge is conserved

    • Kirchhoff’s loop rule:
      • The sum of changes in potential in any closed path (loop) is zero
  • Kirchhoff’s laws can be used to generate a set of linear equations
  • Problem-solving strategy for Kirchhoff’s rules
    • Label points in the circuit diagram using lowercase letters
    • Locate junctions in the circuit and label them with currents and directions into and out of it
      • Any direction is sufficient as long as at least one current points into the junction and and at least one points out
      • The result for the current may be negative, since it could be opposite conventional flow, but the answer is still correct
    • Choose the loops in the circuit
      • Each component must be in at least one loop, but they may be in multiple loops
      • Use the minimum amount of loops such that each component is covered once, without repeating loops
    • Apply the junction rule
      • Some junctions shouldn’t be included
      • Each current should be included in a junction and thus a junction equation
      • Don’t use nodes that are not linearly independent
        • Don’t use nodes that contain the same information
    • Apply the loop rule
      • When a resistor is traversed in the same direction as the current flow, the voltage drop is subtracted
      • When a resistor is traversed in the opposite direction as the current flow, the voltage drop is added
      • When a voltage source is traversed from the negative terminal to the positive terminal, the voltage drop is added
      • When a voltage source is traversed from the positive terminal to the negative terminal, the voltage drop is subtracted
  • Multiple voltage sources can be connected in series, parallel or a combination of the two
  • The terminal voltage of batteries in series is the sum of the individual terminal voltages
  • The current can thus be found
  • In series, a large emf is produced, but the internal resistance is additive
  • In parallel, the terminal voltage is related to the equivalent internal resistance
  • The current can thus be found
  • In parallel, the same emf is produced, but the internal resistance is reduced and a larger current can be produced

Electrical Measuring Instruments

  • Voltmeters are instruments used to measure voltage
    • Placed in parallel with the component
    • Has very large resistance as to not modify the circuit
      • 10 megaohms in cheap ones to 10 gigaohms in expensive ones
  • Ammeters are instruments used to measure current
    • Placed in series with the component
    • Has very low, negligible, resistance as to not modify the circuit
      • Fraction of a milliohm
    • Usually contains a fuse for very high currents
  • Ohmmeters are instruments used to measure the resistance of a component or device
    • Based on Ohm’s law
    • Traditional ohmmeters have an internal voltage source
      • Produces a current, which is measured with a galvanometer and Ohm’s law is used to find the resistance
    • Modern digital ohmmeters use a constant current source
      • The voltage drop is measured and the resistance is found using Ohm’s law
    • The component of interest should be isolated from the circuit
      • Otherwise, the equivalent resistance would be measured
    • Ohmmeters shouldn’t be connected to a live circuit
      • Can damage the meter
  • Meters can be either analog or digital
    • Analog meters use information represented by a physical quantity
    • Digital meters convert analog information to binary and use it to run a digital display
