Work, Energy, and Power

Work

  • Work is done on an object when energy is transferred to it
  • Work is done when a force is exerted on an object that undergoes a displacement because of it
  • Mathematically, work is the integral of force along the path of the displacement with respect to displacement
    • Line integral of force with respect to displacement
  • For a constant force, angle and displacement, work can be defined as the dot product of force and displacement
  • The maximum work is done by a force when it’s parallel to the displacement
  • Zero work is done when the force is perpendicular to the displacement
  • The unit of work is the joule (J) (Newton metres)
  • Usually, the normal force does not do any work
  • The work done against a force is the negative of the work done by the force

Kinetic Energy

  • Energy is the ability to do work
  • Kinetic energy is the energy of motion
    • An object in motion has kinetic energy
    • System of particles (kinetic energy is the sum of the kinetic energy of the individual particles)
    • In terms of momentum:
  • The unit of kinetic energy and energy in general is the joule
  • Since velocity is relative, kinetic energy is also relative
  • Kinetic energy is always positive

Work-Energy Theorem

  • Net work is the sum of all work done on an object
  • The work-energy theorem states that net work is equal to change in kinetic energy
  • Problem solving strategy for work-energy theorem
    • Draw a free-body diagram
    • Determine which forces do work over the displacement
    • Add the work done by each force (net work)
    • Set the net work equal to the change in kinetic energy and solve

Conservative and Non-Conservative Forces

  • For non-conservative/dissipative forces, work done by them depends on the path the object takes
    • Path independent
    • Takes energy away from the system
    • Friction, air resistance
  • For conservative forces, work done by them depends only on the start and end positions
    • Path dependent
    • Work done by a conservative force over a closed path is zero
      • Circle in the middle of the integral sign indicates a line integral over a closed path
    • Gravitational force, spring force, electrostatic force

Conservation of Energy

  • A conserved quantity can be transformed, but not created or destroyed
  • Energy is conserved
  • Mechanical energy is the sum of kinetic and potential energies
  • The mechanical energy of a particle stays constant unless external or non-conservative forces do work on it
    • When external/non-conservative forces do work on it, the change in mechanical energy is equal to the work done by the non-conservative forces
  • In the absence of work done by non-conservative forces, mechanical energy remains constant
  • Problem solving strategy for conservation of energy
    • Identify the system to be studied
    • Identify the forces that do work on the system
    • Determine if each force is conservative or non-conservative
    • If the force is non-conservative, the work done by it must be added as change in mechanical energy
    • For each conservative force that does work, choose a reference point and determine the potential energy function for it
    • Apply the principle of mechanical energy conservation
  • When taking into account nonconservative work:
  • Often, energy-based approaches can give better models than dynamics or kinematics based approaches
  • Position as a function of time can be solved for using energy

Potential Energy Diagrams and Stability

  • A potential energy diagram is a graph of potential energy with respect to position
    • A potential energy diagram for an object in free fall
    • The line at energy E represents the constant mechanical energy
    • As the object’s height changes, the kinetic and potential energy is divided by the line
    • There is also a maximum potential energy and height, since kinetic energy is always positive
    • U≤EU \le EU≤E﻿
  • For an object in free fall, equations can be derived using a potential energy diagram
    • Initial velocity needed to reach maximum height
  • For a spring, equations can be derived using a potential energy diagram
    • Initial velocity needed to reach maximum displacement
  • An equilibrium point is when the acceleration is zero
    • Relative minimum in a potential energy diagram is a stable equilibrium point
    • Relative maximum in a potential energy diagram is an unstable equilibrium point

Sources of Energy

  • Other forms of energy
    • Thermal energy is the internal kinetic energy from atoms and molecules in an object
      • Heat energy is the energy transferred from a hot object to a colder one
    • Electrical energy is energy from electricity
    • Chemical energy is potential energy arising from molecular structure
    • Electromagnetic/radiant energy is energy from electromagnetic rays
    • Nuclear energy is energy released from the atomic nucleus
