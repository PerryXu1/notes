Geometric Optics and Image Formation

  • The field of geometric optics is dominated by geometric constructions like ray diagrams
  • For light interacting with objects much larger than its wavelength, light can be treated as a ray

Images Formed by Plane Mirrors

  • A plane mirror is a flat mirror
    • Images formed by a mirror are the same size as the object, behind the mirror, and oriented the same way as the object
    • We can illustrate this with a ray diagram
  • A virtual image is formed by light rays behind the mirror
    • Cannot be projected onto a screen
    • Rays appear to originate from a common point behind the mirror
  • A real image is formed by light rays that physically go through the image
    • Can be projected onto a screen
  • Both real and virtual images can be seen
  • The object distance dod_odo​﻿ is the distance from the mirror to the object
  • The image distance did_idi​﻿ is the distance from the mirror to the image
  • For a plane mirror:
    • Opposite signs since the object and image are on different sides of the mirror
  • When an object is placed in front of multiple mirrors, you may see multiple images
  • When an object is placed in front of two mirrors, images will be seen in both mirrors
  • If the mirrors can parallel to each other and the object is not placed at the midpoint, infinite images are theoretically generated
    • Images are seen in both mirrors and the image in the front mirror acts as an object for the second mirror, creating an image of an image
      • This process repeats
    • Each reflection reverses front and back
  • Infinite reflections may terminate
    • Two mirrors at right angles form 3 images

Spherical Mirrors

  • A curved mirror can form images that differ in size from the object
    • May also form in front or behind the mirror
  • In general, all curved surfaces will form an image
    • Some may be distorted beyond recognition
  • 2 general forms of spherical mirrors
    • Concave
    • Convex
  • A convex mirror has its reflecting surface on the outside of the sphere
  • A concave mirror has its reflecting surface on the inside of the sphere
  • The axis of symmetry of optical devices is often called the principal axis or optical axis
    • For a spherical mirror, the optical axis passes through both the mirror’s center of curvature and the mirror’s vertex
  • In a parabolic mirror, all rays travelling parallel to the optical axis are reflected so they converge at a point; this point is the focal point
  • For spherical mirrors, there is no well-defined focal point
    • This is called spherical aberration
    • Spherical aberration results in a blurred image of an extended object
    • For a spherical mirror that is small relative to its radius of curvature, the spherical aberration is negligible
      • The focal point is well defined
      • W can also define the distance along the optical axis from the mirror’s vertex to the focal point as the focal length
    • A convex spherical mirror small relative to its radius of curvature has a virtual focal point; the focal point appears to be behind the mirror
  • If the angle between the optical axis and the line from the center of curvature to the point a light ray hits the mirror is small, the focal length of a concave spherical mirror is half its radius of curvature
    • The small angle approximation (AKA paraxial approximation) assumes that all rays are paraxial rays, which make a small angle with the optical axis and are at a distance much smaller than the radius of curvature from the optical axis
  • We use ray-tracing to find the location of an image formed by a spherical mirror
  • To locate the image of an object, we need at least two points of the image
    • Each point requires at least two rays from a point on the object
    • We construct their reflected rays
    • The corresponding point is located where the reflected rays intersect
  • We focus on four principal rays with reflections that are easy to construct
  • Principal rays:
    1. From the top of the object and travels parallel to the optical axis
      • The reflected ray passes through the focal point
        • For a concave mirror, the reflection of principal ray 1 goes through focal point FFF﻿
        • For a convex mirror, the backwards extension of the reflection of principal ray 1 goes through the focal point FFF﻿
          • The focus is virtual
    1. From the top of the object on the line through the focal point and reflected parallel to the optical axis
    1. From the top of the object on the line through the center of curvature and reflected back the same way
    1. From the top of the object to the vertex and reflected symmetrically across the optical axis
  • Where the rays intersect is the image point
  • We need 2/4 of these lines
  • The mirror equation relates the focal length to the object and image distances
  • Sign conventions for spherical mirrors
    • Focal length fff﻿ is positive for concave mirrors and negative for convex mirrors
    • The image distance did_idi​﻿ is positive for real images and negative for virtual images
  • Image and object heights are related to image and object distances
  • Magnification is related to the ratios of object and image heights/distances

  • The distortion of an image due to a focal point not being well defined is called aberration
    • 2 types of aberration include spherical aberration and coma
  • The farther away from the optical axis the rays strike, the worse the spherical mirror approximates a parabolic mirror
    • The rays are not focused at the same point
    • Causes the blurring of an extended object; spherical aberration
    • Only for spherical mirrors
  • Coma (AKA comatic aberration) is an effect similar to spherical aberration that arises when incoming rays are not parallel to the optical axis
    • Since the rays are not parallel to the optical axis, they are focused at different heights and focal lengths
    • Due to the symmetry of the mirror, this creates circles of light on the focal plane
    • Occurs for both parabolic and spherical mirrors

Thin Lenses

  • A lens is a material shaped to use refraction to focus light in a specific way
    • Same vocabulary as mirrors
    • Vertex is where the optical axis (axis of symmetry) intersects the lens surface
  • A convex or converging lens is shaped so all light rays that enter parallel to its optical axis is focused to a single point on the opposite side of the lens
    • Thicker in the middle
  • A concave or diverging lens is shaped so that all rays that enter it parallel to its optical axis are diverged
    • Thinner in the middle
  • For a converging lens, the point where the rays cross is the focal point FFF﻿ of the lens
  • For a diverging lens, the point where the refracted rays appear to originate is the focal point of the lens
    • Virtual
  • A lens is a thin lens if its thickness ttt﻿ is much less than the radii of curvature of both surfaces R1R_1R1​﻿ and R2R_2R2​﻿
  • In a thin lens, light rays that pass through the center must be undeviated
  • The paths of light rays are reversible

The Eye

  • The eye can generate very rich and clear images
    • Eyes often need correction to reach “normal vision”
  • Diagram of an Eye
    • The cornea and the lens form a system that approximates a thin lens

  • For clear vision, a real image must be projected onto the retina, which is light sensitive and at a fixed distance from the lens
  • The center of the image is projected on the fovea, which has the greatest light receptor density and the greatest acuity (sharpness)
  • The pupil, which varies in opening radius, allows the eye to detect light from the lowest observable to 101010^{10}1010﻿ times greater
  • The greatest change in refractive index occurs at the air-cornea interface
  • The cornea, which acts as a converging lens, provides most of the focusing power of the eye
  • The lens itself provides the finer focus
  • The image formed is inverted and the brain inverts them again to make them upright
  • The ciliary muscles adjust eye shape to focus on objects of different distances
    • Accommodation
  • The nearest point an object can be placed so the eye can form a clear image on the retina is called the near point
  • The farthest point an object can be placed so the eye can form a clear image on the retina is called the far point
  • A person with normal vision can see clearly from distances ranging from 25 centimetres to essentially infinity
    • Near point increases with age

The Camera

  • A modern camera uses a charge-coupled device (CCD) detector
    • a CCD is a semiconductor chip that records images as a matrix of tiny pixels
    • Each pixel is in a “bin” in the surface
    • Each pixel detects the intensity of light impinging on it and images are colored by putting red blue and green filters over the pixels
    • At highest resolution, each CCD pixel is 1 pixel of the image
      • Can be decreased in resolution by “binning” several CCD pixels into one
  • The underlying physics of how a camera works is basic optics
    • Single lens with an object distance much greater than the lens’s focal length
  • In a cell phone, the lens and CCD are fixed relative to each other, so focusing becomes hard
    • A phone camera has a very small focal length, which means that the image distance is approximately equal to the object distance no matter the object distance
  • A conventional camera instead uses a moveable lens to change the lens-to-image distance
    • Creates better quality photographs
