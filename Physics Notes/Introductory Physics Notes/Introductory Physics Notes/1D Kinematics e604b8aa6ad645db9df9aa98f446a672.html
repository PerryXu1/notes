Position, Displacement, and Average Velocity

  • Position (x) is where an object is at any particular time
  • Position must be defined relative to a frame of reference
  • A frame of reference is an arbitrary set of axes from which position and motion are described
  • In most coordinate systems, motion to the right is positive and motion to the left is negative
  • Displacement (Δx) is how far an object is from its original position; change in position
  • Displacement is a vector quantity
  • Displacement is the difference between the final (xfx_fxf​﻿) and initial position (xix_ixi​﻿)
  • The SI unit of displacement is the metre
  • The total displacement is the sum of all individual displacements
  • Distance is a scalar quantity and refers to how much an object has travelled
  • Distance is the magnitude (absolute value) of displacement
  • Speed is a scalar quantity that refers to distance over time;
  • Average speed is total distance divided by elapsed time
  • Velocity is a vector quantity that refers to the rate of displacement over time
  • Average velocity is the rate of displacement between 2 points in time
  • The average velocity (vavgv_{avg}vavg​﻿) can be thought of as the displacement divided by the elapsed time (Δt\Delta tΔt﻿)

Instantaneous Velocity and Speed

  • The instantaneous velocity of an object is the rate of displacement over time at a certain instant
  • Average speed is not necessarily the same as the magnitude of the average velocity
  • However, the instantaneous speed is the magnitude of instantaneous velocity

Average and Instantaneous Acceleration

  • Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity with respect to time
  • Average acceleration is the acceleration between 2 points in time
  • Instantaneous acceleration is the acceleration at a point
  • Units of acceleration are ms2\frac{m}{s^2}s2m​﻿
  • Since acceleration is change in a vector quantity (velocity), acceleration can mean change in speed and/or direction
