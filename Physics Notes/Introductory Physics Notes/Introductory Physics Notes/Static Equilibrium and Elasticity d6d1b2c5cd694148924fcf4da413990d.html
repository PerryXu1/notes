Static Equilibrium and Elasticity

Conditions for Static Equilibrium

  • An object is in equilibrium when its linear and angular acceleration is zero
    • An object is in static equilibrium when, relative to our reference frame, it’s at rest
    • An object is in dynamic equilibrium when, relative to our reference frame, it’s in motion
  • Since linear and angular acceleration is zero, there’s no net force or torque
  • When an external force is applied to a body at its center of mass, the body translates with no rotation
  • When the center of mass is located off the axis of rotation, a net gravitational torque will be exerted on the object
    • The net gravitational torque’s magnitude depends on how far away from the pivot the center of mass is located
  • An object is stable if its center of gravity is over its base of support
  • The critical angle is the angle at which the center of gravity is directly over the edge of the base of support
  • Problem solving strategy for static equilibrium
    • Identify the object to be analyzed
    • Draw a free-body diagram for the object
    • Set up the conditions of equilibrium for the object (0 angular or linear acceleration)
    • Solve
    • Check the realism and units of the answer

Stress, Strain, and Elastic Modulus

  • Forces can also affect an object’s shape
  • Deformation is the change in shape of an object due to the application of a force
  • In physics, 2 terms describe the forces on objects undergoing deformation
    • Stress is describes the magnitude of the forces that cause deformation
      • Force per unit area
    • Strain describes deformation due to stress
      • Given as a fractional change in either length (tensile stress), volume (bulk stress), or geometry (shear stress)
      • Dimensionless
  • There are different kinds of stress
    • Tensile stress is stress from forces that pull on an object and elongated it
    • Compressive stress is stress from forces that push on an object and causes a compression
    • Bulk stress is stress due to forces acting on all sides
    • Shear stress is stress due to forces that act tangentially to the surface
  • There are different kinds of strain
    • Tensile strain is caused by tensile stress
    • Compressive strain is caused by compressive stress
    • Bulk strain is caused by bulk stress
    • Shear strain is caused by shear stress
  • The unit of stress is the pascal (Pa)
    • 1 pascal is 1 newton per metre squared
  • Other units are also used:
    • psi (pounds per square inch): 1 psi = 6895 Pa
    • atm (used for bulk stress): 1 atm = 1.013 105 Pa
  • An object under stress becomes deformed
  • The greater the stress, the greater the strain, but the relationship is not necessarily linear
  • When stress is sufficiently low, stress and strain are linear
  • The elastic modulus for tensile stress is called Young’s modulus
  • The elastic modulus for bulk stress is called the bulk modulus
  • The elastic modulus for shear stress is called the shear modulus
  • Elastic moduli are empirically determined
  • Tensile or compressive stress and strain occur when 2 antiparallel forces of equal magnitude act along 1 of the object’s dimension, such that the object does not move
  • Tensile and compressive stress change in the length of the object
  • ΔL\Delta LΔL﻿ is positive when there is tensile strain and negative when there is compressive strain
  • Tensile stress is the ratio of the force acting perpendicular to the cross-sectional area to the cross-sectional area
  • Tensile strain is the ratio of the change in length to the initial length
  • Compressive stress and strain use the magnitude of the equations
  • Young’s modulus (YYY﻿) is the ratio of tensile stress to tensile strain
  • Objects can often experience compressive and tensile stress at the same it (eg. a rod bending)
  • Bulk stress is stress resulting from forces from all directions
  • Bulk stress always tries to decrease the enclosed volume
  • The forces squeezing during bulk stress is always perpendicular to the surface
  • Bulk strain is the ratio of the change in volume to the initial volume
  • The pressure is the scalar ratio of the perpendicular force to the cross-sectional area
  • The pressure of the atmosphere is called the normal pressure (p0p_0p0​﻿)
  • Bulk stress is the increase in pressure over the normal level
  • The bulk modulus is the ratio of the bulk stress to the bulk strain
    • There’s a negative because an increase in pressure results in a decrease in volume
  • Compressibility is the reciprocal of the bulk modulus
  • Compressibility only applies to fluids
    • It describes the change in volume of a fluid per unit increase in pressure
    • Large compressibility indicates a fluid that’s easy to compress
    • Small compressibility indicates a fluid that’s hard to compress
  • Shear stress and strain do not apply to fluids; only to solid objects
  • Shear deformation occurs when 2 antiparallel forces with equal magnitude are applied to opposite surfaces of a solid object, tangent to the sides
  • Shear strain is the ratio of the largest displacement (Δx) to the transverse distance (L0)
  • Shear stress is due to forces parallel to the surface; ratio of parallel force to area of the surface it’s acting tangentially to
  • The shear modulus (SSS﻿) is the ratio of shear stress to shear strain

Elasticity and Plasticity

  • Elasticity is the tendency of solid objects to return to their original shape after the stress causing their deformation is removed
    • An object is elastic if it returns to its original shape after stress is removed
  • Under high stress, when that stress is removed, the object will relax to a different size or shape; permanently deformed
    • The elastic limit is the stress value beyond which the object is permanently deformed
  • The linearity limit is the stress value beyond which the relationship between stress and strain is no longer linear
  • An object exhibits plastic behaviour beyond the elastic limit
  • The relationship between stress and strain can be graphed with a stress-strain diagram
  • The value at which an object fractures is called the breaking stress (AKA ultimate stress)
