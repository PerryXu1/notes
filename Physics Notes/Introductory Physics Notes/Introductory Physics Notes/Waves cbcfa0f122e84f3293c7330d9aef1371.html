Waves

Travelling Waves

  • Mechanical waves are disturbances that move through a medium
  • The energy transferred by the wave is proportional to the amplitude squared
  • Oscillations are heavily related to waves
    • Oscillations can generate waves
    • Many terms and equations from oscillations carry over
  • A wave is a disturbance that propagates from the place it was created
  • A medium is a substance that mechanical waves propagate through
    • Produces an elastic restoring force when deformed
  • There are 3 basic types of waves: mechanical, electromagnetic and matter
    • Mechanical waves are governed by Newton’s laws
      • Require a medium
      • Transfer energy and momentum without transferring mass
        • Energy and momentum are transferred with motion
        • Mass oscillates around an equilibrium point
      • Water waves, sound waves, seismic waves, etc.
    • Electromagnetic waves are related to oscillations in the electric and magnetic fields
      • Does not require a medium
      • Travel through a vacuum at the speed of light, c
      • Gamma rays, X-rays, ultraviolet waves, visible light, infrared waves, microwaves, radio waves
    • Matter waves are a central part of quantum mechanics
      • Associated with particles such as protons, electrons, etc.
      • All matter has wave-like properties
  • This chapter only focuses on mechanical waves
  • Mechanical waves exhibit common properties like frequency, period, amplitude, wavelength and energy
  • The simplest type of mechanical waves repeat themselves for several cycles
    • Associated with simple harmonic oscillators
    • Can be easily modelled using a combinations of sine/cosine functions
  • For instance, in a surface water wave, the water oscillates up and down whereas the disturbance moves horizontally
  • A wave’s highest point is a crest and its lowest point is a trough
  • The amount of time for one complete oscillation is the period TTT﻿
  • The wave’s frequency f ff﻿ is the amount of waves that pass through a point per unit time
    • Frequency is measured in Hertz (s−1s^{-1}s−1﻿)
  • The length of a wave is called a wavelength
    • Represented by the Greek letter lambda (λλλ﻿)
    • Measured between 2 points with the same height and slope
      • Usually between consecutive crests/troughs
  • The period is also the time for one wavelength to pass through a point
  • A wave’s amplitude (A) is the maximum displacement of the medium from its equilibrium position
    • For a symmetrical wave, the crests are at +A+A +A﻿ above equilibrium while the troughs are at −A-A−A﻿
  • The propagation velocity (vvv﻿) is related to the wavelength and frequency of the wave:
  • A transverse wave’s displacement is perpendicular to its direction of motion
  • A longitudinal wave’s displacement is parallel to its direction of motion
    • The size of the disturbance is its amplitude
    • The amplitude is independent of its speed of propagation
    • In a spring, the wave is seen as the compression and expansions of the spring
    • A point horizontally oscillates around its equilibrium point, giving an amplitude
    • The height of the point doesn’t change
  • Waves can be transverse, longitudinal, or a combination of both
  • The frequency of a wave produced by an oscillating driving force is equal to the frequency of the driving force

Energy and Power of a Wave

  • The amount of energy in a wave is dependent on its frequency and amplitude
    • The larger the amplitude, the larger the displacement
    • The larger the frequency, the more wavelengths per unit time
  • The average rate of energy transfer in a mechanical wave is proportional to both the amplitude squared and the frequency squared
    • In electromagnetic waves, the rate of energy transfer is proportional to the amplitude squared, but independent of the frequency
  • When deriving the energy of a wave, the energy of a wave can be separated into wavelengths
  • The kinetic energy of a single wavelength is proportional to both the amplitude and frequency squared
  • The potential energy of a single wavelength is equal to the kinetic energy
  • The total energy of a wavelength is the sum of kinetic and potential energy
  • The time-averaged power of a wave is the total energy passing a point divided by the time
  • If the wave has a constant velocity, the time it takes for a wavelength to pass a point is one period
  • Thus, the average power is the energy of a wavelength over the period:
  • Since the wave velocity is equal to the wavelength over the period, the average power of a wave is proportional to its velocity
  • The velocity of a wave on a stretched string is also dependent on the linear density, so the time-averaged power is proportional to the square root of the linear density
  • The intensity (power per unit area) of a wave is also important, as power over a larger area isn’t as destructive
  • Intensity is measured in watts per square metre
  • For a spherical wave, the intensity decreases by a factor of the radius squared

Interference of Waves

  • When a wave meets the boundary of a medium, it reflects
    • The wave prior to meeting the boundary is called the incident wave
    • The wave after meeting the boundary is called the reflected wave
  • Waves will react differently depending on the properties of the medium boundary
    • A fixed boundary condition exists when the medium boundary is fixed in place and cannot move
    • A free boundary condition exists when the medium boundary is free to move
  • For a fixed boundary condition, the reflected wave is inverted (reflected 180 degrees out of phase with respect to the incident wave)
  • For a free boundary condition, the reflected wave is reflected normally (in phase)
  • For both types of boundary conditions, the amplitudes of the incident and reflected waves are equal
  • Sometimes, the boundary condition is neither fixed nor free
    • A low linear mass density string can be attached to a higher mass linear density string or vice versa
  • When a low linear mass density string is attached to a higher linear mass density string:
    • The reflected wave is out of phase with the incident wave
    • There’s also a transmitted wave in phase with the incident wave
    • The amplitudes of the transmitted and reflected wave are smaller than that of the incident
    • If the tension is same in both strings, the wave speed is higher in the low linear mass density string
  • When a high linear mass density string is attached to a lower linear mass density string:
    • The reflected and transmitted waves are both in phase with the incident wave
    • The amplitudes of the reflected and transmitted waves are both less than that of the incident
    • If the tension is the same in both strings, the wave speed is higher in the string with the lower linear mass density
  • Interference is the phenomenon that occurs when 2 waves travelling in the same medium meet
  • The principle of superposition states that if two or more travelling waves combine at the same point, the resulting position of the mass element at that point is the algebraic sum of the position due to the individual waves
    • This is exhibited by many waves, like waves on a string, sound waves, surface water waves, etc.
    • EM waves also obey it, but the magnetic and electric fields are added instead of displacement
  • Waves that obey the principle of superposition are linear
  • Waves that don’t obey the superposition principle are nonlinear
  • Linear waves normally have small amplitudes with respect to their wavelength, as too large an amplitude would distort the medium past a linear restoring force
  • Waves can interfere constructively or destructively
    • 2 waves at the same point exactly in phase with amplitude AAA﻿ constructively interfere to create a wave with amplitude 2A2A2A﻿
    • 2 waves at the same point 180 degrees out of phase with amplitude AAA﻿ destructively interfere to create a wave with amplitude 000﻿
  • In superposition, most waves have both constructive and destructive interference
    • The resultant waves can be complex
  • Many examples in physics consists of two sine waves that differ only by a phase shift
    • The resultant wave has amplitude AR=2Acos⁡ϕ2A_R = 2A \cos \tfrac{\phi}{2}AR​=2Acos2ϕ​﻿
    • The wave number and angular frequency are the same
      • Thus, the period, wavelength, and frequency is the same
    • The phase shift is halved

    • Such stationary waves are called standing waves

    • If the two waves have equal amplitude and wavelengths, they will alternate between constructive and destructive interference, creating a standing wave
