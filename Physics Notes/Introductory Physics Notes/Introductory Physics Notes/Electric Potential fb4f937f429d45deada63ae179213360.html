Electric Potential and Potential Difference

  • The electric potential VVV﻿ (or just potential) is the electric potential energy per unit charge
  • The change in potential energy can therefore also be written in terms of a potential difference ΔV\Delta VΔV﻿ between 2 points
  • The unit of electric potential/potential difference is a Volt
    • 1 V=1 JC1 \text{ V} = 1 \space \dfrac{\text{J}}{\text{C}}1 V=1 CJ​﻿
  • Electric potential difference is also known as voltage
    • Potential difference between 2 points
  • Whereas electric potential energy is energy, voltage is the energy per unit charge
  • The electron-volt is a very small unit of energy used in submicroscopic scales
    • Energy given to 1 electron accelerated through a voltage of 1 volt
  • As for every other form of energy, electrical potential energy is conserved and we can utilize conservation of energy to solve problems
  • The voltage can also be related to the underlying electric field
    • Path independent
  • For a uniform electric field, the voltage is directly proportional to the electric field

  • There is no difference in electric potential along a constant radius from a point charge

Equipotential Surfaces and Conductors

  • Electric potentials can be represented pictorially
    • We use lines of constant potential called equipotential surfaces in 3 dimensions and equipotential lines in 2 dimensions
  • A point charge has spherical equipotential surfaces
  • Equipotential lines are always perpendicular to electric field lines
    • If the electric field lines are given, we can draw the equipotential lines
    • If the equipotential lines are given, we can draw the electric field lines
  • Since the electric field must be perpendicular to any conductor’s surface, a conductor is an equipotential surface under static conditions
    • If a conductor’s surface is not equipotential, charge will flow
  • A conductor can be fixed at zero volts by connection it to the Earth with a good conducting
    • Called grounding
  • Since a conductor is an equipotential, it can replace any equipotential surface
  • In 3 dimensions, positive charges create a “hill” while negative charges create a sink
  • For parallel conducting plates, equipotentials are equally spaced and parallel
  • Charge density on a conductor varies based on radius of curvature
  • In a conductor’s surface region where radius of curvature is larger, charge density and electric field will be smaller
  • In a conductor’s surface region where radius of curvature is small, charge density and electric field will be larger
